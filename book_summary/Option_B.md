# Option B — by Adam Grant & Sheryl Sandberg
### Facing adversity, building resilience, and finding joy after loss

---

## ELEVATOR SUMMARY
When Sheryl Sandberg's husband Dave Goldberg died suddenly in 2015, she was consumed by grief that felt permanent and total. Option B — written with psychologist Adam Grant — is the book that came out of her recovery. It is part memoir, part science, arguing that resilience is not a fixed trait but a muscle that can be built, and that people are far more capable of bouncing forward — not just back — than they believe in their darkest moments.

---

## CORE ARGUMENT
The book's organizing principle is drawn from a phrase a friend said to Sandberg when she was devastated that Dave couldn't attend a father-child event: "Option A is not available. Let's kick the shit out of Option B." This becomes a framework for how people adapt to loss, trauma, or setback — not by replacing what was lost, but by building a new version of a meaningful life.

Grant brings the research; Sandberg brings the raw emotional truth. Together they argue that three false beliefs — called the **Three P's** (Personalization, Pervasiveness, Permanence) — extend suffering unnecessarily. And that resilience is strengthened through specific practices: finding meaning, allowing post-traumatic growth, rebuilding identity, and creating communities of support rather than suffering alone.

---

## KEY IDEAS

**1. The Three P's — the Enemies of Recovery**
Martin Seligman's research, which Grant applies directly, identifies three cognitive patterns that deepen and extend suffering:
- **Personalization**: "This is my fault." Taking excessive blame for events outside one's control.
- **Pervasiveness**: "This ruins everything." Letting grief bleed into every part of life.
- **Permanence**: "I will always feel this way." Treating current pain as a permanent state.

Countering these three beliefs — not denying the grief, but questioning the absoluteness — is one of the most actionable things the book offers.

**2. Post-Traumatic Growth**
Grant synthesizes research showing that many people don't just return to their previous baseline after trauma — they surpass it in specific dimensions: personal strength, new possibilities, relating to others, appreciation for life, and spiritual/existential change. This doesn't minimize pain; it reframes what's possible on the other side of it.

**3. Resilience as Community, Not Just Individual**
One of the book's most important arguments is that resilience is social, not solitary. Sandberg describes how friends didn't know what to say and so said nothing — compounding isolation. Grant's research shows that acknowledging grief directly ("I know today is hard, and I'm here") is far more helpful than avoidance. Resilience is built *with* people, not independently of them.

**4. Finding Meaning — Not Happiness**
The book draws on Viktor Frankl and post-trauma psychology to argue that the goal after loss is not to return to happiness, but to find *meaning* — which is a different and more durable state. Meaning can be found in honoring the person lost, in channeling grief into purpose, or in the simple act of helping others who are suffering similarly.

**5. Resilience for Children**
Grant devotes significant space to how to build resilience in children: normalizing negative emotions rather than bypassing them, modeling recovery rather than invulnerability, and teaching children to distinguish between controllable and uncontrollable events.

**6. Resilience at Work**
Organizations often handle employee grief poorly — an uncomfortable silence, or a brief acknowledgment before returning to performance expectations. Grant argues for workplaces that make room for authentic human emotion, noting that psychological safety and grief support actually improve long-term productivity and loyalty.

## NOTABLE EXAMPLES / CASE STUDIES

- **Sheryl Sandberg's grief journal** — she began writing daily after Dave's death. The raw passages she includes are among the most honest accounts of acute grief in any mainstream business-adjacent book.
- **Bouncing forward after childhood trauma** — Grant cites studies of Holocaust survivors, 9/11 responders, and cancer patients who reported unexpected growth; the research is careful to acknowledge this doesn't happen for everyone and cannot be forced.
- **The elephant in the room** — Sandberg describes the experience of friends who stopped mentioning Dave's name entirely, thinking they were protecting her. In fact, it deepened her isolation. The research-backed antidote: say the name.

---

## CRITICAL ANALYSIS

**Strengths:** The emotional authenticity Sandberg brings is genuinely rare in books of this type — she is not performing recovery, she is documenting it in real time. The Three P's framework is immediately applicable. The social/communal framing of resilience is an important corrective to the over-individualized "grit" narrative.

**Limitations:** The book is most directly applicable to people with strong existing support systems, financial security, and social capital. Sandberg herself acknowledges she had extraordinary resources — professional support, a network of world-class friends, material security — that most people facing loss do not have. The universal claims about resilience can occasionally feel tone-deaf to these structural realities.

**Best for:** Anyone navigating loss, grief, or major setback; managers supporting employees through hardship; people who want to become better friends to those who are suffering.

**Connects to:** Viktor Frankl's *Man's Search for Meaning* (meaning-making under suffering), Brené Brown's *Rising Strong* (vulnerability and recovery), Martin Seligman's *Flourish* (positive psychology foundations).

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS / ACTION ITEMS
- When someone you know is grieving, say something — even "I don't know what to say, but I'm here." Silence is worse than imperfect words.
- When you're suffering, check your thinking for the Three P's. Ask: is this my fault entirely? Does it affect everything? Will I feel this way forever?
- Don't aim to return to who you were before loss — aim to discover who you can become because of it.
- Say the names of people who have died. It helps those who loved them.
- Build the habit of finding three moments of joy per day — not to bypass grief, but to coexist with it.
