# Originals — by Adam Grant
### How non-conformists change the world — and how you can too

---

## ELEVATOR SUMMARY
Most people assume original thinkers are bold, fearless visionaries who act on impulse. Adam Grant dismantles this myth. Originals — people who champion new ideas and drive change — are often doubt-ridden, risk-averse in key ways, and strategically patient. The book is a practical guide to generating better ideas, overcoming fear, and building coalitions that actually make change happen.

---

## CORE ARGUMENT
Grant's central argument is that originality is not a personality type — it's a set of learnable behaviors. The people who drive the most change are not those with the fewest doubts; they are those who act *despite* doubt. More surprisingly, the most successful originals are strategic procrastinators, calculated risk-takers (not reckless ones), and skilled coalition-builders who understand timing and audience.

The book challenges three dominant myths: that originals are born, not made; that they are fearless; and that speed is always an advantage. Grant shows that the most disruptive innovators hedge their personal risk, take their time with execution, and obsess over *which* ideas to champion rather than generating infinite new ones.

---

## KEY IDEAS

**1. The Quantity-Quality Paradox**
The best predictor of producing one great idea is producing *many* ideas. Even creative geniuses have low hit rates — what separates them is volume. Grant cites research showing that Mozart, Einstein, and Picasso all had prolific output, and their masterworks were not isolated flashes but products of relentless generation.

**2. The Procrastination Advantage**
Counterintuitively, moderate procrastination improves creativity. When you start a task and then let it sit — rather than diving straight through — your mind continues working on it unconsciously. Grant's research shows that people who procrastinate moderately generate more creative solutions than both those who rush to complete and those who delay indefinitely.

**3. Strategic Risk Management**
Originals are not risk-lovers — they are risk-balancers. Many of history's boldest entrepreneurs (including Warby Parker's founders) kept their day jobs while launching their ventures. They took creative risks while maintaining financial stability. Grant argues this "balance portfolio" approach — risk in one domain, security in another — is far more common among successful originals than the "burn the boats" narrative suggests.

**4. Timing and the First-Mover Myth**
First movers don't always win. Grant documents cases where late entrants — who waited to understand the market, learn from early mistakes, and refine their approach — outperformed pioneers. Being original doesn't mean being first; it means being better in a way that's well-timed.

**5. Managing Fear and Doubt**
Originals experience intense doubt, but they reframe it. Instead of "I'm afraid of failing," successful originals ask "I'm afraid of *not trying*." Grant describes two types of doubt: self-doubt (paralyzing) and idea-doubt (energizing). The key is to direct doubt at the idea — which prompts improvement — rather than at yourself.

**6. Coalition Building and Audience Reading**
Even brilliant ideas fail without the right advocates. Grant studies how originals earn champions within organizations — often by framing ideas in terms of existing values ("this is consistent with what we already believe") rather than as radical departures. Timing the audience — knowing when a system is ripe for change — is as important as the idea itself.

**7. Raising Original Children**
In a late chapter, Grant applies the research to parenting: original thinking is nurtured by moral reasoning over rules, exposure to diverse role models, and homes that emphasize *values* ("we're a family that helps others") rather than labels ("you're a good kid").

---

## NOTABLE EXAMPLES / CASE STUDIES

- **Warby Parker founders** — kept their day jobs until the last moment. Grant uses them to debunk the "all-in" mythology around entrepreneurship.
- **Seinfeld's pilot rejection** — NBC executives rated it poorly; it nearly wasn't made. The story illustrates how even world-class ideas face skepticism, and how persistence and coalition-building matter more than initial reception.
- **Carmen Medina at the CIA** — a mid-level analyst who spent years trying to introduce digital information sharing and was repeatedly dismissed. Grant analyzes *why* her timing finally worked and what she did differently.

---

## CRITICAL ANALYSIS

**Strengths:** The procrastination finding is genuinely surprising and well-sourced. The coalition-building and timing sections are among the most practically useful in any business book. Grant writes with real intellectual honesty — he acknowledges his own doubts and mistakes throughout.

**Limitations:** The book occasionally conflates "being original" with "being right" — not all dissent is valuable. The advice is most applicable to people already in positions of relative safety (employed, educated, with networks); for truly marginalized voices, the barriers to being heard are much higher than strategy alone can solve.

**Best for:** Professionals with ideas they haven't acted on; managers trying to build more creative cultures; parents thinking about how to raise independent thinkers.

**Connects to:** Ed Catmull's *Creativity, Inc.* (building creative organizations), Daniel Pink's *Drive* (intrinsic motivation), Malcolm Gladwell's *Outliers* (contextual success factors).

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS / ACTION ITEMS
- Generate more ideas — don't filter too early. Volume is the friend of quality.
- If you're procrastinating on a creative problem, don't fight it. Let the idea incubate, then return with structure.
- Identify one big risk in your life and ask: where can I create stability to offset it?
- When pitching a new idea inside an organization, frame it in terms of existing values, not against them.
- Find your "tempered radical" — an insider advocate who can carry your idea through resistant corridors.
- Replace self-doubt with idea-doubt: interrogate the idea, not yourself.
