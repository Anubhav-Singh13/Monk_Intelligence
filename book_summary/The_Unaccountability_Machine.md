# The Unaccountability Machine — by Dan Davies
### Why big organizations always seem to escape blame — and why that's a feature, not a bug

---

## ELEVATOR SUMMARY

Modern institutions — corporations, governments, regulators, banks — are not failing at accountability. They are *succeeding* at unaccountability. Dan Davies argues that the very structures we build to manage complexity systematically destroy the feedback loops that would otherwise force decision-makers to face the consequences of their choices. The result is a world where disasters happen, nobody is responsible, and nothing changes.

---

## CORE ARGUMENT

Drawing heavily on the cybernetics tradition — particularly the work of Stafford Beer and his concept of the "viable system model" — Davies argues that accountability is fundamentally an *information problem*. Organisations absorb and filter information as they grow; by the time a decision reaches the top, it has been sanitised, aggregated, and stripped of the feedback that would tell a leader whether their choices are working. This is not corruption. It is the normal operation of management hierarchy.

The deeper claim is structural: the division of labour that makes large organisations effective is the *same* mechanism that makes accountability impossible. When a decision is the product of a hundred people across a dozen departments, the question "who is responsible?" stops having a meaningful answer. Responsibility has been laundered through process. Davies calls the resulting entity an "unaccountability machine" — a system that, whether by design or emergence, routes consequences away from decision-makers and deposits them onto customers, employees, taxpayers, or the environment.

The book is also a critique of how we *respond* to unaccountability. Public inquiries, regulatory fines, and leadership reshuffles give the appearance of accountability while leaving the underlying machine intact. The fine gets paid; the process that caused the harm continues; the next scandal is already accumulating.

## KEY IDEAS

### 1. The Cybernetics of Control
Davies revives Stafford Beer's mid-twentieth-century management cybernetics to argue that every viable system requires *variety* in its control mechanism matching the variety of the environment it operates in (Ashby's Law of Requisite Variety). Large organisations routinely violate this — their management layers reduce variety, destroying the sensitivity to feedback that would keep behaviour aligned with outcomes. The machine runs, but no one at the top can actually "feel" what it is doing.

### 2. Accountability Sinks
The book's central metaphor: an accountability sink is a structural feature — a committee, a process, an algorithm, a contracted-out function — that absorbs a decision and releases it without any identifiable human author. When the airline overbooks your flight, a yield-management algorithm made that decision; when your loan is denied, a credit-scoring model did it. No one chose to harm you; a *system* did. Davies argues that accountability sinks are not accidents — they are often deliberately constructed to neutralise the political and legal consequences of decisions that someone in the organisation *did* choose to make.

### 3. The Franchise System as Accountability Laundry
One of the book's sharpest case studies: the franchise model (fast food, retail chains, gig platforms) is shown to be a nearly perfect unaccountability machine. The franchisor sets prices, standards, and systems; the franchisee is nominally the employer and the legally responsible party. When workers are underpaid or customers are harmed, the franchisor can disclaim responsibility because it does not "employ" anyone. The structure makes it legally true that no one is in charge — while economically ensuring that one party captures the profits and another absorbs the liability.

### 4. The Purpose of Management is to Preserve the Organisation
Davies argues — following Beer — that the primary function of management in a large organisation is not to achieve its stated goals but to ensure the organisation's *survival*. An organisation that becomes too responsive to external feedback (customer complaints, regulatory signals, market losses) would constantly be reorganising itself, which threatens survival. So management layers exist partly to *buffer* the organisation from information that would force costly change. This reframes "incompetent management" as often being *effective* management — effective at protecting the institution at the expense of its nominal purpose.

### 5. Metrics as Accountability Substitutes
When genuine accountability is absent, metrics rush in to fill the gap. But Davies shows how metrics systematically distort the behaviour they are meant to measure (Goodhart's Law in practice). Police forces optimise for recorded crime rates, hospitals optimise for waiting-list targets, schools optimise for league-table positions — and in each case the metric diverges from the underlying reality it was meant to track. Metrics create the *appearance* of accountability while dismantling the conditions that would produce genuine feedback.

### 6. The "Decision" That Never Was
Davies makes an important epistemological point: many institutional outcomes are not the product of any decision at all. They emerge from the accumulation of many small choices, each individually innocuous, none of which can be isolated as the cause of the harm. The 2008 financial crisis was not decided by anyone. It was the emergent output of thousands of incentive structures operating simultaneously. This matters because it means that punishment — fining, firing, prosecuting individuals — does not address the cause. The machine that produced the crisis remains.

### 7. Regulatory Capture as System Equilibrium
Regulation is typically proposed as the solution to unaccountability, but Davies argues that regulators are subject to the same dynamics. Regulators exist within an information environment largely controlled by the regulated. Over time, regulators come to share the worldview of the industries they oversee — not through corruption, but because the information that reaches them is filtered through those industries. Regulatory capture is not a failure state. It is the equilibrium state of a system with mismatched information variety.

## NOTABLE EXAMPLES & CASE STUDIES

**The Horizon IT Scandal (Post Office, UK):** Davies uses this as a near-perfect case study. The Post Office prosecuted hundreds of subpostmasters for "theft" based on accounting errors produced by Fujitsu's Horizon software. No single person decided to destroy those lives; a cascade of institutional processes — legal, managerial, technical — produced the outcome while every individual within it could reasonably claim they were "just following procedure." The accountability sink swallowed everything.

**Stafford Beer and Allende's Chile (Project Cybersyn):** One of the book's most unexpected chapters traces Beer's attempt to build a genuine real-time feedback system for the Chilean economy under Allende — a system that would give government genuine variety to match economic complexity. Davies uses this to show what accountability *could* look like if systems were actually designed for it — and why such designs are almost never built, because they would make it impossible for management to insulate itself from consequences.

**The 2008 Financial Crisis:** Securitisation is dissected as an accountability sink of spectacular scale. Mortgage originators sold risk to packagers, packagers sold it to CDO managers, CDO managers sold it to money market funds. At each stage, the link between the original lending decision and its consequences was severed. When the mortgages defaulted, accountability had been so thoroughly laundered that no chain of causation could reach back to any decision-maker. The fines ultimately paid by banks were paid by shareholders, not by the individuals who designed the structures.

## CRITICAL ANALYSIS

**Strengths:**
Davies writes with unusual clarity for a book trafficking in cybernetics and systems theory. The accountability sink concept is genuinely original as a *named* phenomenon, even if the underlying dynamic has been observed before. The book is at its best when it is concrete — the Post Office, the franchise model, the financial crisis — and the cybernetics scaffolding, while dense at times, earns its place by providing a unified explanatory framework rather than just a catalogue of corporate malfeasance. Davies is also intellectually honest: he acknowledges that the structures he critiques often exist for legitimate reasons (coordinating complexity is hard) before showing how they metastasise.

**Limitations:**
The book's solutions are thin relative to the diagnosis. Davies is better at explaining *why* unaccountability is structural than at telling us what to do about it. The closing chapters gesture toward redesigning feedback loops and restoring "requisite variety" to regulatory systems, but these prescriptions remain abstract. Critics might also note that the cybernetics framework, while illuminating, risks over-unifying — not every instance of apparent unaccountability is equally explained by information-filtering; some is just power protecting itself, which requires different remedies. The book is also substantially UK-centric in its examples, and some case studies (particularly the financial crisis) cover ground that readers of other post-2008 literature will find familiar.

**Best for:** Policy professionals, regulators, management consultants, students of organisational theory, and anyone trying to understand why institutional disasters keep happening without anyone being blamed.

**Skip if:** You want a prescriptive reform agenda, or if you've read extensively in the post-2008 financial crisis literature and want something more novel in its empirical scope.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

- **Look for the accountability sink, not the villain.** When an institutional harm occurs, the question "who did this?" often has no answer. Ask instead: what structure absorbed the decision and released it without an author? That is where reform needs to go.

- **Metrics are not accountability.** Measuring outcomes and holding people to targets creates incentives to game the metric, not improve the underlying reality. Genuine accountability requires feedback loops that cannot be gamed — which means consequences must connect to the actual decision-makers, not to proxies.

- **The franchise/contractor/algorithm structure is a political choice.** When organisations externalise liability through contracting, platformisation, or automation, they are making a deliberate decision about who bears risk. Calling it "market structure" obscures this choice.

- **Regulatory design matters more than regulatory intent.** A regulator that lacks information variety cannot be effective regardless of how motivated it is. The design question is: how do you ensure regulators receive unfiltered feedback from the regulated environment?

- **Survival instinct is the enemy of learning.** Organisations that prioritise self-continuity over responsiveness to feedback will systematically fail to learn from their mistakes. If you are inside such an organisation, the only accountability that will reach you is external — and you should plan accordingly.

- **Demand structural reform, not individual punishment.** Fines and sackings after disasters are typically theatre. If the structure that produced the harm remains, the harm will recur. The test of any accountability response is whether it changes the information flows and incentive structures — not whether someone went to jail.

## FURTHER READING

1. **Brain of the Firm** — Stafford Beer: The source text for the viable system model that underpins Davies's argument. Dense, but the cybernetics framework is here in full.
2. **Normal Accidents** — Charles Perrow: The complementary argument that complex, tightly coupled systems will inevitably produce catastrophic failures — regardless of individual competence.
3. **The Blunders of Our Governments** — Anthony King & Ivor Crewe: A data-rich companion to Davies for readers who want the UK policy-failure evidence base in forensic detail.
