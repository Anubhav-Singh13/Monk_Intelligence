# Chokepoint Capitalism — by Rebecca Giblin & Cory Doctorow
### How Big Tech and Big Content built toll booths between creators and their audiences — and what to do about it.

---

## ELEVATOR SUMMARY

*Chokepoint Capitalism* argues that the creative economy is broken not because creators are untalented or unlucky, but because monopolistic corporations have engineered structural "chokepoints" — bottlenecks that force every creator to pass through a single gatekeeper to reach their audience. Spotify, Amazon KDP, YouTube, the Big Five publishers, and a handful of radio conglomerates have used acquisitions, contracts, and platform design to capture the pipes through which music, books, and writing flow — and then toll those pipes ruthlessly. The book is both a diagnosis and a manual for resistance.

---

## CORE ARGUMENT

Giblin and Doctorow's central claim is deceptively simple: **when buyers or sellers become concentrated enough to control a market's chokepoint, they can extract value from everyone else in the system — indefinitely.** This is not a side effect of capitalism; it is a deliberate strategy. They call it "chokepoint capitalism" to distinguish it from the standard narrative about market competition. The usual story is that creators fail because they can't find audiences; the real story is that intermediaries have positioned themselves to capture most of the value even when creators *do* find audiences.

The mechanism works in two directions. On the seller side, platforms capture the distribution channel so thoroughly that not selling through them means near-zero reach (Amazon controls ~80% of US ebook sales; Spotify is the default for audio streaming). On the buyer side, record labels, publishers, and studios have consolidated so completely that creators have almost nowhere else to take their work. These two concentrations — buyer monopsony and seller monopoly — form a vice that squeezes creators from both ends. The authors trace this through music, books, news, and live events, showing that the pattern is consistent across every creative sector.

## KEY IDEAS

### 1. The Chokepoint Playbook
The book opens with a precise anatomy of how chokepoints are built. Step one: acquire distribution until you are unavoidable. Step two: impose contracts that lock creators in (often standard-form, non-negotiable). Step three: use your position to set prices and royalty rates that funnel surplus upward. Doctorow and Giblin trace Amazon's ebook strategy here in forensic detail. Amazon launched the Kindle at $9.99 per ebook — deliberately below cost — to establish price expectations that would be nearly impossible to reverse. When publishers tried to raise prices through the "agency model," Amazon retaliated by removing buy buttons from their catalogs, briefly "disappearing" thousands of titles. The lesson: once a platform has conditioned buyers to a price point, it can use that expectation as a weapon against the suppliers it depends on.

### 2. Streaming's Royalty Shell Game
The music streaming section is the book's most viscerally infuriating. Spotify's royalty model sounds reasonable in the abstract — artists receive a share of total streams. In practice, it is structured so that the money flows first to major labels (who own equity in Spotify and negotiated minimum guaranteed payments before launch), then to publishers, and only then — if anything remains — to artists. The authors cite the case of Spotify's 2018 IPO: the three major labels (Universal, Sony, Warner) received tens of millions in shares as part of their licensing deals. When those shares were sold, the profits did not flow to artists — they were retained by the labels, despite being generated by artists' work. Giblin and Doctorow call this "the iceberg": creators see the royalty statement above the waterline; the bulk of the value transfer happens beneath it.

They also dissect the "pro-rata" royalty pool system. Under this model, every stream on Spotify goes into a single global pool, which is then divided proportionally by total streams. This means a listener who plays nothing but jazz all month generates royalties that are partly paid to pop artists they never listened to. It systematically advantages high-volume catalog owners (i.e., major labels) over independent artists. A "user-centric" model — where each listener's subscription fee flows only to the artists they actually played — would distribute money more equitably. Spotify has resisted implementing it.

### 3. Amazon and the Kindle Lockbox
The ebook chapter is a case study in how a platform can capture a market by subsidizing early adoption and then tightening the vise. Amazon's KDP Select program offers self-published authors significantly higher royalties (70% vs. 35%) and access to Kindle Unlimited — but only if the book is *exclusively* sold on Amazon. Exclusivity clauses are the chokepoint's enforcement mechanism: they prevent authors from building any alternative distribution and make it economically irrational to leave, even as Amazon's terms worsen.

The authors note a specific contractual clause they describe as particularly predatory: Amazon can change the royalty rate it pays KDP authors at any time, with 30 days' notice. Authors who have built their income around Kindle have no meaningful recourse. The switching cost — losing Kindle Unlimited access, losing Amazon search visibility — is engineered to be prohibitive.

### 4. The Radio Royalty Scandal
One of the book's most underreported examples involves terrestrial radio in the United States. Unlike virtually every other country, US law does not require radio stations to pay royalties to performers (only to songwriters). This exemption was originally carved out as a subsidy to a nascent radio industry. Decades later, iHeartMedia — which owns over 860 stations and went through bankruptcy in 2018 — still benefits from it. Giblin and Doctorow point out that iHeartMedia's business model depends on paying musicians nothing while monetizing their recordings. The exemption persists because of lobbying, not merit; it represents a direct wealth transfer from artists to a concentrated broadcast oligopoly.

### 5. News and the Platform Squeeze
The book extends the chokepoint framework to journalism. Google and Facebook collectively capture approximately 60–70 cents of every digital advertising dollar, leaving publishers with a shrinking share even as traffic (and therefore content consumption) grows. The authors analyze Australia's News Media Bargaining Code, which attempted to force platforms to negotiate payment with publishers. Google and Facebook responded by threatening to leave Australia entirely — a threat that revealed just how much leverage a chokepoint owner has: the credible ability to disappear the market for everyone else. The Australian law ultimately passed in a weakened form, but the episode illustrated that legislative intervention is possible, if incomplete.

### 6. Attention as a Chokepoint
Giblin and Doctorow make a subtler argument that deserves emphasis: the chokepoint is not just distribution — it is *attention*. Algorithmic recommendation systems on Spotify, YouTube, and Amazon are not neutral. They are tuned to maximize engagement metrics that benefit the platform. Spotify's editorial playlists (curated by Spotify employees) can make or break an album's first-week numbers. YouTube's recommendation engine drives a significant portion of total viewing hours. When creators optimize for algorithmic favor — shorter books, faster tempos, clickable thumbnails — they are not just responding to audience taste. They are responding to the platform's design choices, which reflect the platform's economic interests, not the creator's or the audience's.

### 7. The Remedies
The book's second half is its most distinctive contribution: a concrete policy and legal toolkit. The proposals include:

- **Transparency mandates**: Require platforms to publish royalty calculations in auditable form. Creators currently cannot verify their own royalty statements.
- **Reversion rights reform**: Strengthen the "termination right" in US copyright law (Section 203), which theoretically allows authors to reclaim copyrights after 35 years. In practice, the right is so procedurally complex that most authors never exercise it.
- **Collective licensing**: Borrow from the model of ASCAP/BMI (music performing rights organizations) to give creators collective bargaining power in sectors where they currently negotiate as isolated individuals.
- **Interoperability requirements**: Force dominant platforms to allow data portability, so a creator's audience on Spotify can follow them to a competitor. This is the structural remedy — it breaks the network-effect moat that makes platforms hard to leave.
- **Antitrust enforcement**: The authors call for a revival of structural antitrust — not just mergers, but behavioral remedies like forced divestiture of platforms that vertically integrate distribution and content ownership.

## NOTABLE EXAMPLES & CASE STUDIES

### The Amazon "Buy Button" Incident (2014)
When Hachette, one of the Big Five publishers, disputed ebook pricing with Amazon, Amazon removed the pre-order buttons from Hachette authors' pages — effectively erasing those books from the world's largest bookstore. Authors including James Patterson and Malcolm Gladwell published open letters. Amazon offered to set up a fund to compensate Hachette authors during the dispute — conditioned on Hachette matching the contribution. The episode revealed that Amazon could use authors as hostages in a negotiation they had no part in. The authors use this to illustrate a key point: when your distributor controls 80% of your market, your publisher's dispute with that distributor becomes your crisis.

### Spotify's "Hate Content" Policy (2018)
Spotify briefly introduced a policy to demote artists accused of "hate content or hateful conduct" from playlists and recommendations — without due process or clear criteria. R. Kelly and XXXTentacion were targeted. The policy was quickly retracted after backlash, but Giblin and Doctorow use it to illustrate a different point: Spotify's algorithmic levers are powerful enough to effectively "cancel" an artist's revenue without any legal action. A platform that controls recommendation is a platform that controls careers.

### Ticketmaster and the Live Event Chokepoint
Though not the book's focus, the authors extend their framework to Live Nation/Ticketmaster — the merged entity that controls venue booking, ticketing, artist management, and promotion. A venue that refuses to use Ticketmaster risks losing access to Live Nation-managed artists. An artist who refuses to work with Live Nation risks losing access to Live Nation-owned venues. The chokepoint here is complete vertical integration: the same company owns every link in the chain. The authors note this is precisely the structure that antitrust law was designed to prevent but failed to.

## CRITICAL ANALYSIS

### Strengths
The book's greatest achievement is making structural economics viscerally legible. Giblin and Doctorow do not just assert that Spotify pays too little — they show *mechanically* how the royalty system was designed, who negotiated it, and who benefits. The forensic specificity is rare in books aimed at a general audience. The policy section is equally strong: unlike most "platform power is bad" books, this one ends with tractable proposals, not despair.

The framing of "chokepoint" is genuinely clarifying. It reorients the conversation away from "creators vs. consumers" or "piracy vs. protection" toward the structural question of who controls the pipe. This is the right question, and the authors ask it consistently across sectors.

### Limitations
The book occasionally treats the remedies as more proven than they are. The collective licensing proposals, for instance, have real-world analogues (ASCAP, the Authors Guild) that the authors acknowledge but do not fully interrogate. ASCAP itself has faced criticism for favoring major publishers in its distribution of collected royalties — it is not obvious that collective licensing solves the power-imbalance problem rather than shifting it.

The digital-first framing also underweights the ways creators have *gained* leverage from platforms. Self-publishing on Amazon KDP, imperfect as it is, gives authors a path to readers that did not exist under the pre-Amazon gatekeeping model of traditional publishing. The authors are right that Amazon has built a chokepoint; they are less careful about acknowledging what creators lost access to before that chokepoint existed.

The tone can tip from advocacy into polemic in places, particularly in the Doctorow-voiced sections. Readers who arrive skeptical of the "big tech is always bad" frame may find their skepticism confirmed rather than engaged.

**Best For**: Policy advocates, creators navigating platform contracts, anyone studying platform economics, media industry professionals, and readers interested in the intersection of IP law and market structure.

**Connects To**: *The Chaos Machine* (Max Fisher) on algorithmic harm; *Move Fast and Break Things* (Jonathan Taplin) on platform monopoly in music; *Winners Take All* (Anand Giridharadas) on elite capture of social value; *Four Futures* (Peter Frase) on labor under algorithmic capitalism.

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

- The problem with creator pay is structural, not individual — streaming more, writing better, or building a bigger following does not escape the chokepoint.
- Exclusivity clauses in platform contracts are the primary enforcement mechanism of chokepoints; read them carefully before signing.
- Collective action — collective licensing, union-equivalent organizations, coordinated refusals — is the only proven counter to monopsony power.
- Transparency about royalty calculations is a precondition for any reform; without it, creators cannot even verify they are being cheated.
- Interoperability (the ability to move your audience) is more valuable than higher royalty rates; fight for portability.
- Antitrust is not enough on its own — structural remedies need to accompany behavioral ones to durably break a chokepoint.
