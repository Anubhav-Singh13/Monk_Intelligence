## Wanting: The Power of Mimetic Desire in Everyday Life — by Luke Burgis
### We don't choose what we want — we copy it from others, and that hidden process shapes everything.

---

**ELEVATOR SUMMARY**

Luke Burgis resurrects the mostly forgotten theory of René Girard — a 20th-century French philosopher who argued that human desire is fundamentally imitative, not autonomous. We don't want things because of their intrinsic value; we want them because someone else wants them. This "mimetic desire" operates beneath our awareness, driving rivalry, status anxiety, and a restless dissatisfaction that no achievement can fully cure. Burgis translates Girard's dense academic work into a practical framework for diagnosing and redirecting your own wanting.

---

**CORE ARGUMENT**

The modern self-help industry is built on a flattering lie: you have authentic desires, and the goal is to discover and pursue them. Burgis argues the opposite. Drawing on Girard's concept of *mimesis* (imitation), he contends that desire is triangular — not a straight line between you and an object, but a triangle involving you, the object, and a *model* whose wanting mediates yours. The model signals that something is worth wanting. Strip away the model, and the desire often evaporates.

This isn't a minor psychological quirk; it's the engine of culture, markets, conflict, and social hierarchy. When many people share the same model, they converge on the same desires — and since we can't all have the same thing, mimetic desire produces rivalry. Competitors in the same industry, siblings in the same family, colleagues on the same team: the closer two people are, the more likely they are to mirror each other's desires and become rivals. Girard called this *mimetic rivalry*, and Burgis shows how it explains phenomena from Silicon Valley funding frenzies to college admissions arms races to social media's anxiety machine.

The book's constructive claim: once you can see the mimetic forces shaping you, you can choose *thick desires* — wants rooted in your own history, values, and what Burgis calls your "fulfillment story" — over *thin desires* imported wholesale from your social environment.

---

**KEY IDEAS**

**1. Mimetic Desire — The Triangle**
Desire is not dyadic (me → object) but triadic (me → model → object). The model — a peer, a celebrity, a fictional character, a LinkedIn connection — tacitly endorses the object by wanting it. Burgis calls this person a *model-mediator*. External mediators are far away (celebrities, historical figures) and relatively harmless. Internal mediators are close — colleagues, friends, people in your direct social orbit — and dangerous, because proximity turns admiration into rivalry almost automatically.

**2. Mimetic Rivalry and the Double Bind**
When model and subject share the same social world, they begin to mirror each other's desires reciprocally. Each person's wanting intensifies the other's. The object becomes almost irrelevant — what matters is winning. Burgis illustrates this through the "mimetic double bind": "Be like me / Don't be like me." Parents, bosses, and mentors unconsciously communicate both messages, inciting imitation while punishing it when the imitator gets too close.

**3. Romantic Lies and Novelistic Truth**
Girard distinguished between *romantic* accounts of desire (we freely choose what we love based on its intrinsic qualities) and *novelistic* truth (great literature — Cervantes, Stendhal, Dostoevsky, Proust — reveals the mimetic machinery underneath). Burgis extends this: most of our self-narratives are romantic lies. We believe we chose our career, our partner, our ambitions organically. Novelistic honesty means asking: who was I imitating when I made this choice?

**4. The Scapegoat Mechanism**
When mimetic rivalry reaches a fever pitch in a group, the group unconsciously unites by redirecting its aggression onto a single victim — the scapegoat. This releases tension and temporarily restores peace. Girard saw this as the foundation of archaic religion and sacrifice; Burgis applies it to organizational culture (the fired executive everyone blames), political cycles, and cancel culture. The scapegoat is rarely actually responsible for the group's disorder.

**5. Freshmen vs. Seniors — Gravity of Models**
Not all models pull equally. Burgis uses the metaphor of "gravity wells": models in your immediate social stratum exert the most pull. A Harvard freshman feels more mimetic pressure from other Harvard freshmen than from Elon Musk. This is why peer comparison is so destabilizing — and why deliberately choosing *distant* or *transcendent* models (historical figures, ideals, people from radically different contexts) can liberate desire from the rivalry spiral.

**6. Thick vs. Thin Desires**
Thin desires are shallow, easily adopted from the social environment, and leave you empty once satisfied. Thick desires are connected to your history, your values, and a coherent long-term vision of the person you want to become. Burgis argues most people live almost entirely in thin desire without knowing it, chasing objects validated by their mimetic models while their deeper wants go unexamined and undeveloped.

**7. The Fulfillment Story**
Burgis's central practical tool: write down a narrative of moments when you felt genuinely fulfilled — not successful, not admired, but *fulfilled*. These moments reveal what you actually value, independent of external validation. From this story you can identify the "north star" goals that organize your wanting around something thicker than what your social environment is currently celebrating.

**NOTABLE EXAMPLES / CASE STUDIES**

**Silicon Valley's Mimetic Tornado**: Burgis spent years in the startup world and describes how venture-backed companies converge on the same ideas not because those ideas are best, but because the same small network of investors and founders model the same desires for each other. The "hot sector" of any given year is often a mimetic phenomenon, not a rational market signal. When everyone wants what everyone else wants, valuations detach from fundamentals.

**René Girard at Stanford**: Girard was a largely ignored literary scholar until Peter Thiel (then a student) encountered him. Thiel became one of Girard's most important evangelists — and also one of the most self-conscious anti-mimetic thinkers in business ("competition is for losers" is a Girardian insight). Burgis uses Thiel as a complex case: someone who understood mimetic desire deeply, yet arguably built PayPal and Palantir partly through intense mimetic rivalry with Google and the defense establishment.

**The College Admissions Machine**: A clean example of mimetic escalation. As more students apply to top schools, more schools become desirable simply because they're competitive. The degree's value becomes increasingly mediated by selectivity (how many others want it) rather than by what students actually learn. Students who "win" often feel hollow — they wanted what their models wanted, not what they wanted.

---

**CRITICAL ANALYSIS**

**Strengths**: Girard's core insight is genuinely illuminating, and Burgis translates it with clarity and personal honesty. The framework has real explanatory power across domains — markets, relationships, organizational dynamics, political scapegoating — in a way that most social psychology frameworks don't. The distinction between thick and thin desire is practically useful in a way that Girard's own academic work was not. Burgis is also refreshingly willing to apply the theory to himself, describing his own mimetic traps in the startup world.

**Limitations**: The theory risks over-extension — not every desire is mimetic, and Burgis sometimes strains to make the framework fit. The "fulfillment story" exercise, while sensible, is underdeveloped as a methodology; it's not obvious how to distinguish a genuinely thick desire from a thin one that's simply been rationalized. The book also sidesteps the hardest Girardian territory: if mimesis is this fundamental, can you ever truly escape it, or do you just find better models to copy? Burgis gestures at transcendence but doesn't fully reckon with the determinism his own framework implies.

**Best for**: Knowledge workers, founders, and anyone experiencing the peculiar dissatisfaction of achieving what they worked toward and feeling nothing. Also strong for managers and leaders who want to understand how rivalry poisons teams.

**Connects to**: *Zero to One* by Peter Thiel (Girardian business strategy); *The Status Game* by Will Storr (status drives social behavior); *Man's Search for Meaning* by Viktor Frankl (choosing one's own values against external pressure); *Decoded* by Phil Barden (mimesis in consumer behavior); Girard's own *Violence and the Sacred* for the uncompressed source.

**KEY TAKEAWAYS / ACTION ITEMS**

- **Audit your models**: List the five people whose lives you find yourself most tracking. What exactly do you want that they have — and would you still want it if they didn't have it?
- **Write your fulfillment story**: Identify 3–5 moments of genuine fulfillment (not pride or status), describe them in detail, and extract the common elements. Use these to test whether a current goal is thick or thin.
- **Distance your models deliberately**: When you feel intense rivalry or envy toward someone close to you, recognize it as a signal that you're in a mimetic double bind. Increase psychological (or literal) distance. Find models further away.
- **Name the scapegoat dynamic**: When a group collectively turns on someone, ask what collective desire the group is displacing. The target is rarely the real issue.
- **Slow down desire acquisition**: Before committing to a new goal, ask: *When did I first want this? Who else around me wants it? Would I still want it if I were the only person who did?*
- **Choose a "transcendent model"**: Identify one person — historical, fictional, or simply far from your social orbit — whose values you genuinely admire independent of their status. Use them as a reference point when mimetic pressure intensifies.
