# Rebuilding Milo: The Lifter's Guide to Fixing Common Injuries and Building a Strong Foundation for Enhancing Performance
## by Dr. Aaron Horschig & Dr. Kevin Sonthana

### Pain is not a medical mystery to be diagnosed on a screen — it is a movement problem to be solved with your own body.

---

## ELEVATOR SUMMARY

*Rebuilding Milo* is a comprehensive self-rehabilitation manual written by physical therapist and Squat University founder Dr. Aaron Horschig for strength athletes — powerlifters, Olympic weightlifters, and CrossFitters — who are dealing with pain. The book's central argument is that most common training-related injuries (back, hip, knee, shoulder, elbow, ankle) are not medical emergencies requiring surgery or painkillers but movement problems with identifiable causes and systematic, exercise-based solutions. Horschig gives readers the diagnostic tools, exercise progressions, and conceptual framework to understand and fix their own pain — and stop the destructive cycle of "lift, lift, break."

---

## CORE ARGUMENT

The book builds on a single foundational premise: every strength athlete who trains hard enough will get hurt. The question is not whether pain will occur but whether you have the tools to address it when it does. Horschig argues that the medical system as it currently operates fails strength athletes in two critical ways: it treats symptoms rather than causes (prescribing rest, painkillers, and surgery instead of targeted movement), and it relies too heavily on imaging (MRI, X-ray) that shows anatomy but not function.

His alternative framework rests on three pillars. First, **screen before treating** — identify the specific movement, load, or posture that triggers your pain through a structured diagnostic process before applying any exercises. Second, **restore movement through progressive loading** — injured tissues heal through systematic exercise, not rest; the rehab progression moves from isometrics (pain-free contraction without movement) to heavy slow resistance training to sport-specific reintegration. Third, **address the root cause** — pain always has a reason, usually a combination of mobility restrictions, strength deficits, technique errors, and training overload. Fix the root cause; don't tape over the hole in the tire.

The book borrows its title and inspiration from the ancient Greek Olympian Milo of Croton, who built legendary strength by carrying a calf daily until it became a full-grown bull — the original story of progressive overload. Horschig reframes rehabilitation as its own progressive overload: start where the body is, load it carefully, build from there.

## KEY IDEAS

### 1. MRI Findings Do Not Equal Pain Causes
One of the book's most important contributions is its debunking of the dominant imaging-diagnosis model. Horschig cites research showing that disc bulges are present in approximately one-third of healthy, pain-free 20-year-olds and increase with every decade of life. A 2006 study of 200 pain-free individuals found that in 84% of those who later developed pain, MRI scans showed no change from their original baseline — the "abnormality" was already there and wasn't causing pain. An MRI shows anatomy in a single static posture; it cannot determine whether a disc bulge is a new wound or a 20-year-old scar, and it cannot show function. The correct evaluation process must establish context through movement testing: which specific movements, loads, and postures trigger pain — and which don't.

### 2. The Three-Stage Rehabilitation Pyramid
Every chapter follows a consistent rehabilitation architecture:
- **Stage 1 — Reduce Pain**: Identify and temporarily avoid the specific provocative movement or load. Perform isometric exercises (muscle contraction without joint movement) to decrease pain and begin restoring neuromuscular control. Isometrics have been shown to reduce pain for up to 45 minutes post-exercise and counteract cortical inhibition (the brain's tendency to suppress a painful muscle's output).
- **Stage 2 — Rebuild Capacity**: Progress to Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) training — traditional strengthening exercises performed slowly (3-second eccentric, 3-second concentric) with loads exceeding 70% of 1RM. Tendons, unlike muscles, require heavy loads to adapt and heal. HSR builds load-bearing capacity without using the tendon as a spring, which would overload recovering tissue.
- **Stage 3 — Bridge to Performance**: Reintegrate sport-specific movements (squats, deadlifts, Olympic lifts) using technique modifications (block pulls, paused deadlifts, goblet squats before back squats) and progressive load, eventually returning to full training load.

### 3. The Load Tolerance Model
Every muscle and tendon has a current load tolerance — a threshold beyond which injury occurs. Most training injuries are not freak accidents but the result of training load consistently exceeding recovery capacity. The goal of rehabilitation is not to rest until the pain disappears (which actually lowers load tolerance and guarantees reinjury on return) but to find the correct amount of load that stimulates healing without exceeding the tissue's current capacity. This requires modifying training — reducing volume, frequency, or intensity — rather than stopping entirely.

### 4. Mobility, Stability, and Technique as a System
Horschig demonstrates consistently across all six injury chapters that pain at any joint is almost never caused by that joint alone. Hip pain is often driven by thoracic spine stiffness, weak glutes, or limited hip internal rotation. Knee pain is often a consequence of valgus collapse from poor hip abductor strength or limited ankle dorsiflexion. Shoulder pain frequently traces to tight lats, stiff pecs, poor scapular stability, or thoracic kyphosis. This interdependence means effective rehabilitation requires a systematic screen that looks both locally (the site of pain) and globally (the joints and muscle groups above and below).

### 5. The McGill Big Three for Spinal Stability
For back pain specifically, Horschig gives significant attention to Dr. Stuart McGill's foundational research on spine mechanics and core stability. The Big Three exercises — the Bird Dog, the Side Plank, and the Modified Curl-Up — are prescribed as the foundation of back rehabilitation because they build core stability through multiple planes of motion while minimizing compressive and shear forces on the spine. Core stability training must progress through four movement categories: Push (squat, sled push), Pull (deadlift, inverted row), Carry (suitcase carry), and Anti-Rotation (Pallof press, one-arm row) — because athletes who only train sagittal-plane movements leave lateral and rotational stability undertrained.

### 6. The Valsalva Maneuver and Intra-Abdominal Pressure
Across multiple chapters, Horschig addresses a near-universal technical error: athletes being taught to exhale during the lifting phase ("breathe in on the way down, out on the way up"). He explains that under heavy load, what actually stabilizes the spine is intra-abdominal pressure (IAP), created by taking a large breath, bracing the core in all directions, and holding that breath through the lift — the Valsalva maneuver. The diaphragm, pelvic floor, and abdominal muscles work as a pressurized cylinder, and releasing air mid-lift collapses this system. This is how the body's natural weightlifting belt works.

### 7. Stop Icing — Move Instead (Chapter 7)
Perhaps the book's most provocative chapter challenges one of sport medicine's oldest conventions. Horschig argues that ice delays healing by preventing inflammatory cells (white blood cells, macrophages, neutrophil) from reaching the injured site. Inflammation is not an enemy — it is Stage 1 of the healing process; it must occur for repair and remodeling to follow. A 2011 study cited in the book found that at 28 days post-injury, regenerating muscle in the non-icing group was 65% larger than in the icing group, with the icing group showing significantly more scarring. Even the originator of the RICE protocol, Harvard physician Dr. Gabe Mirkin, retracted his endorsement in 2013. Swelling is not the problem — failure to evacuate swelling through the lymphatic system is. The lymphatic system is passive (unlike the circulatory system) and can only move fluid through muscle contraction. The prescription: move, as early and as pain-free as possible. For those who cannot move, NMES (neuromuscular electrical stimulation) devices stimulate involuntary muscle contraction to drive lymphatic drainage.

## NOTABLE CASE STUDIES

**Josiah O'Brien — Bilateral Patellar/Quad Tendon Rupture**: In October 2016, competitive powerlifter Josiah O'Brien tore nearly every supporting ligament and tendon in both knees during a 655-pound squat attempt. He developed rhabdomyolysis (kidney failure from severe muscle damage) and spent 12 weeks in straight-leg braces, unable to bend his knees. He required 100+ physical therapy visits over nine months with Horschig to relearn how to walk, run, and jump. He returned to competition less than a year later. The case opens the book as both a cautionary extreme and a proof of concept for systematic rehabilitation.

**Author's Own Knee Injury at National Championships**: Horschig describes developing severe patellar tendon pain during two-a-day training sessions in preparation for the 2011 USA Weightlifting Senior Nationals. His professors told him to "just stop lifting." Instead, he masked the pain with Advil and Icy Hot and competed anyway, placing sixth but underperforming. This experience — the inadequacy of conventional advice, the frustration of a quick-fix mentality — is the catalyst for the book's entire methodology.

**Ryan the CrossFitter (Back Pain)**: Ryan is used throughout the back chapter as a composite case study. He develops back pain during competition, gets an MRI showing a disc bulge, receives a diagnosis of the cause based on imaging alone, is prescribed rest and painkillers, returns prematurely to training, re-injures himself, and is told surgery is likely next. Horschig walks through every failure point in this chain to show how a movement-based diagnostic approach would have produced a faster, more targeted resolution.

## CRITICAL ANALYSIS

**Strengths:**
- *Rebuilding Milo* is unusually rigorous for its genre. Horschig cites peer-reviewed research throughout (not just from exercise science but also orthopedic surgery, sports medicine, and physical therapy) and explains the mechanistic reasoning behind each recommendation. The book does not tell you what to do without explaining why — a rare quality in sports rehabilitation writing.
- The injury-by-injury chapter structure makes it immediately navigable for someone with a specific problem. You don't need to read the whole book; read your chapter.
- The consistent rehabilitation framework (screen → isometrics → HSR → sport-specific reintegration) is genuinely teachable and transferable across body regions. Readers will internalize the structure, not just follow instructions.
- The MRI/imaging critique is evidence-based, well-sourced, and corrects a genuinely widespread misunderstanding in the medical treatment of pain.
- The anti-icing chapter, while initially counterintuitive, is one of the best popular summaries of the current evidence base on cryotherapy and inflammation in print.

**Limitations:**
- The book is written specifically for barbell athletes. The exercises and injury mechanisms assume familiarity with squats, deadlifts, snatches, cleans, and Olympic lifting vocabulary. Readers without this background will find sections inaccessible.
- The book is heavily focused on musculoskeletal overuse and training-related injury. Traumatic injuries, structural pathologies (labral tears, disc extrusions requiring surgery, nerve root compression with neurological symptoms), and non-training pain sources are out of scope and are flagged only with "see a doctor" notes.
- For a 585-page book, the written descriptions of exercises sometimes struggle to replace video instruction. The book includes photographs, but complex movement patterns (hip airplanes, single-leg RDL progressions) benefit considerably from visual demonstration — which is why Horschig's Squat University YouTube channel is effectively a companion resource that the text doesn't quite substitute for.
- The scope differences between chapters are notable. The back pain chapter is the most comprehensive (covering disc pathology, facet joint injuries, stenosis, spondylolisthesis, and technique modifications across multiple lifts). Later chapters, particularly ankle and elbow, are comparatively thinner — though still practical.

**Best for:** Competitive and recreational strength athletes (powerlifters, Olympic weightlifters, CrossFitters, strong lifters generally) dealing with nagging pain who want to understand and fix the problem themselves before or instead of surgery. Also valuable for coaches and physical therapists who work with barbell athletes and want a practical, evidence-based reference.

**Skip if:** You are looking for general fitness rehabilitation, general population pain management, or running/endurance sport injury guidance.

**Connects to:**
- *Back Mechanic* — Stuart McGill: the canonical deep reference on spine mechanics and rehabilitation that Horschig draws on repeatedly throughout the back chapter
- *Becoming a Supple Leopard* — Kelly Starrett: a broader mobility-and-movement framework that complements Horschig's targeted injury approach (Starrett wrote the Foreword)
- *Iced! The Illusionary Treatment Option* — Gary Reinl: the foundational anti-icing text that Horschig's Chapter 7 largely summarizes and endorses

## KEY TAKEAWAYS / ACTION ITEMS

- **Screen first, exercise second.** Before starting any rehab program, identify which specific movements, loads, and postures provoke your pain — and which don't. Treat that information, not the diagnosis on your MRI report.
- **Don't rest completely.** Complete rest lowers load tolerance and ensures reinjury when you return. Find the level of load that stimulates healing without reproducing symptoms, and train there.
- **Start with isometrics.** For any tendon pain (patellar, Achilles, elbow, hip flexor), begin with isometric contractions: hard, sustained (45 seconds), pain-free. These reduce pain, restore neuromuscular control, and begin rebuilding load tolerance without overloading the tissue.
- **Progress to HSR before returning to sports-specific lifts.** Tendons need heavy loads (>70% 1RM) to adapt. Use slow tempo (3-1-3) and high weight for 4 sets of 15 reps, progressing to 12, 10, 8, 6 over weeks before returning to fast or maximal lifting.
- **Look above and below the pain.** Knee pain? Screen your hips and ankles. Shoulder pain? Screen your thoracic spine and lats. Pain is rarely isolated; the cause is usually upstream or downstream.
- **Brace all the way around for heavy lifting.** 360-degree abdominal bracing plus a held breath (Valsalva) creates intra-abdominal pressure — your body's natural weightlifting belt. Exhaling during a heavy lift collapses this system.
- **Stop icing injuries.** Ice temporarily numbs pain but delays healing by blocking inflammatory cells that must reach the injury site. After injury, perform pain-free isometrics or light movement as soon as possible to activate the lymphatic system and begin tissue repair.
- **Modify, don't abandon.** If your back hurts during conventional deadlifts, switch to block pulls. If your knee hurts during full-depth squats, use box squats to limit depth. Stay in the weight room; change the variables until you find what your body can currently handle.

## FURTHER READING

1. **Back Mechanic** — Stuart McGill (2015): The definitive practitioner-level text on spine biomechanics and low-back rehabilitation. Horschig draws on McGill throughout his back chapter; this is the primary source.
2. **Becoming a Supple Leopard** — Kelly Starrett and Glen Cordoza (2nd ed., 2015): A broad mobility and movement framework for athletes that complements *Rebuilding Milo*'s targeted injury approach. Starrett's foreword in this book signals how closely the two philosophies align.
3. **Iced! The Illusionary Treatment Option** — Gary Reinl (2nd ed., 2014): The book that convinced Dr. Gabe Mirkin (inventor of RICE) to retract his endorsement. Essential background for understanding why Chapter 7's anti-icing argument is grounded in decades of accumulating evidence.
