# The Blunders of Our Governments — by Anthony King & Ivor Crewe
### A forensic autopsy of British policy failure — and an uncomfortable answer to why it keeps happening

---

## ELEVATOR SUMMARY

Anthony King and Ivor Crewe spent years cataloguing the most expensive, embarrassing, and consequential policy failures of British government from the 1980s through to the early 2010s. What they found is not incompetence, not bad luck, and not the inevitable complexity of governing. What they found is a repeating pattern — the same dozen or so structural and cultural defects appearing again and again, across governments of every party, producing disasters that were in almost every case predictable and preventable. *The Blunders of Our Governments* is one of the most evidence-rich arguments in print for why democratic governments in the Westminster tradition systematically fail — and what, with significant difficulty, might be done about it.

---

## CORE ARGUMENT

King and Crewe reject two popular explanations for government failure. The first — that politics is simply too complex for anyone to get right — they regard as intellectually lazy and empirically false; governments in comparable democracies manage the same challenges more successfully. The second — that the failures they catalogue were caused by specific bad individuals who happened to be in the wrong place — fails because the same types of failure recur with different people, different parties, and different departments.

Their argument is structural and cultural. The British system of government, as it actually operates, has a series of built-in defects that reliably produce a particular type of blunder: a policy that is poorly designed, inadequately tested, launched too fast, monitored too weakly, and corrected too slowly, at very large cost to taxpayers and often serious harm to citizens. These defects are not accidents of the current government — they are properties of the system: its culture of secrecy, its adversarial political incentives, its lack of policy evaluation infrastructure, its ministerial churn, and its deeply embedded preference for action over analysis.

The book makes a further argument that is rarely stated so clearly: the people at the top of British government — ministers, senior officials — are often genuinely unaware that what they are doing is going wrong. The feedback mechanisms that would tell them are absent, suppressed, or structured in ways that allow denial. The blunders they document are not cynical — they are largely the product of a system that prevents the people running it from knowing what is actually happening.

## KEY IDEAS

### 1. Defining a "Blunder"
King and Crewe are careful about what counts. A policy is a blunder — not a mere failure or a reasonable bet that didn't pay off — when it meets three criteria: it caused significant harm (financial, social, or political); it was avoidable (the information to do better was available); and it resulted from human or institutional error rather than unpredictable circumstances. On this definition, they identify approximately twelve major blunders spanning Conservative and Labour governments, with smaller episodes supporting the same patterns.

### 2. The Syndrome of Errors — Eleven Recurring Defects
The book's intellectual core. Rather than treating each blunder as unique, King and Crewe identify eleven defects that appear in combination across all the cases:

**Cultural and intellectual defects:**
- **Hubris**: Ministers and officials convinced that their judgment is superior, that standard cautions don't apply to them, that this time it will be different.
- **Ministerial musical chairs**: Ministers rarely stay in a department long enough to understand it, let alone to be accountable for outcomes. Average ministerial tenure has fallen dramatically since the mid-twentieth century.
- **Asymmetry of expertise**: Departments negotiating IT contracts, PFI deals, or welfare reforms are negotiating against contractors and lobbyists who have spent decades doing exactly this. The knowledge gap is enormous and structural.
- **Cultural disconnect**: Policy is designed by well-educated, largely metropolitan professionals with limited direct experience of the lives of the people the policy will affect. The result is policies that work in theory and fail in practice.

**Operational defects:**
- **Group-think**: The social dynamics of government — deference to ministers, career incentives for agreement, the absence of genuine devil's advocacy — reliably suppress the dissenting voices that might catch errors before they become disasters.
- **Deficient operational thinking**: British government is good at producing policy documents and terrible at thinking through implementation — the logistics, the IT, the frontline staff capacity, the unintended consequences of the design in contact with real people.
- **Inadequate policy evaluation**: Pilots and evaluations are either not done, done poorly, or done and ignored. The feedback loop between policy outcomes and policy design is extraordinarily weak.
- **Failure to learn from experience**: The same types of errors — botched IT systems, failed privatisations, welfare reform disasters — recur because institutional memory is weak, inquiries produce reports that are not acted upon, and the incentive to acknowledge past failure is minimal.

**Political and accountability defects:**
- **The electoral incentive to announce**: Politicians in a twenty-four-hour media environment face constant pressure to *be seen to act*. Announcing a new initiative is costless and rewarded. Quietly piloting and evaluating a policy before scaling it is invisible and receives no credit.
- **Absence of accountability**: The constitutional doctrine of ministerial responsibility has been largely emptied of content. Ministers rarely resign for the failures of their departments; officials are protected by civil service convention. The accountability that might discipline bad decision-making is largely ceremonial.
- **The British political tradition of strong executive**: The Westminster system concentrates executive power and minimises effective scrutiny. Parliamentary committees are under-resourced; judicial review is limited in scope; freedom of information is resisted. The system lacks the external checks that force governments in other democracies to be more careful.

### 3. The Case Studies — Major Blunders
The book examines a series of disasters in forensic detail. Each illustrates a distinct combination of the eleven defects:

- **The Poll Tax**: Hubris, group-think, cultural disconnect, inadequate piloting. The Conservative government's confidence that a flat-rate local government tax was politically and practically viable, despite repeated warnings from their own officials, produced one of the costliest political and administrative disasters in post-war British history.

- **NHS IT Programme (National Programme for IT / NPfIT)**: Asymmetry of expertise, deficient operational thinking, failure to learn. The largest civilian IT project in history, contracted at extraordinary speed with inadequate specification, produced systems that were never fully implemented at a cost of approximately £10 billion. The same errors that had destroyed previous government IT projects were repeated almost exactly.

- **Individual Learning Accounts**: Speed, fraud vulnerability, inadequate design. The scheme to fund adult learning was opened without adequate safeguards, was immediately exploited by fraudulent providers on a massive scale, and was shut down in humiliation within months.

- **Tax Credits Implementation**: Over-complexity, inadequate operational thinking, cultural disconnect. A policy designed to benefit lower-income families through a complex tax system caused widespread overpayments and subsequent recovery demands that pushed some families into destitution. The complexity that made the policy elegant in design made it disastrous in implementation.

- **The Millennium Dome**: Hubris, the electoral incentive to announce, deficient operational thinking. A prestige project announced and committed to for political reasons, with insufficient time and inadequate planning for what it would actually contain.

- **PFI (Private Finance Initiative)**: Asymmetry of expertise, the British tradition of strong executive, inadequate accountability. Long-term private financing contracts for public infrastructure, negotiated by government departments against specialist contractors, systematically transferred risk to the public while generating extraordinary returns for private investors. The contracts were structured so their true cost was off-balance-sheet and would be borne by future governments.

### 4. The Role of Luck
King and Crewe make a point that is both important and uncomfortable: some policies that were equally poorly designed simply got lucky. The blunders they catalogue are not unique in their design flaws — they are distinguished partly by the absence of the luck that might have saved them. This implies that the rate of latent policy blunder is substantially higher than the visible rate of disaster.

### 5. Comparison with Other Democracies
The book argues — carefully and with acknowledgment of the limits of comparison — that the British system is unusual in the degree to which it concentrates executive power without effective internal or external checks. Nordic democracies, the US federal system, and German coalition governance all produce different types of failure, but they also produce different feedback mechanisms that catch and correct errors more quickly. The British system's particular pathology is speed and confidence: it can move very fast, which can be an advantage, but it cannot easily be made to slow down and check.

## CRITICAL ANALYSIS

**Strengths:**
*The Blunders of Our Governments* is a rare combination: genuinely original empirical research (the authors interviewed hundreds of officials, ministers, and participants) presented with exceptional clarity and structured by a unified analytical framework. The eleven-defect syndrome is not a list of complaints — it is a genuine analytical tool that allows the authors to show why the same types of failure recur regardless of party. The book is also admirably even-handed: Conservative and Labour governments receive equal treatment, and the authors are explicit that they do not regard the failures as ideologically motivated. The case studies are gripping; the Poll Tax and NPfIT chapters in particular are outstanding investigative journalism.

**Limitations:**
The prescriptions are weaker than the diagnosis — a common problem with systemic critiques of political systems. King and Crewe recommend modest reforms (longer ministerial tenure, more effective parliamentary scrutiny, better policy evaluation infrastructure) that are all sensible and all politically unlikely. The book does not grapple seriously with *why* the incentive structures that produce blunders are so durable — which would require a political economy argument the authors largely avoid. Some readers have also noted that the selection of cases, while carefully justified, inevitably reflects editorial judgment; the authors acknowledge they are not producing a comprehensive dataset. The book is also exclusively focused on Westminster-system Britain, and its lessons for other systems require translation.

**What the book does not address:**
The relationship between democratic accountability and good policy design. The electoral system that rewards announcement and punishes cautious piloting is not a defect that can be fixed by administrative reform — it reflects genuine democratic pressures. King and Crewe identify the problem without fully confronting the tension between democratic responsiveness and effective governance.

**Best for:** Civil servants, policy professionals, politicians, public administration academics, and anyone trying to understand why British government produces the outcomes it does. Also essential reading for anyone designing accountability mechanisms in large institutions.

**Skip if:** You want a theoretical framework for government failure rather than an empirically grounded British case study. The book is rich in evidence but somewhat light in comparative political theory.

**Connects to:**
- *The Unaccountability Machine* — Dan Davies (structural analysis of why accountability fails in large organisations generally)
- *Normal Accidents* — Perrow (the systemic inevitability of failure in complex institutions)
- *Thinking in Systems* — Donella Meadows (the feedback-loop framework that underpins why the defects King and Crewe describe are so persistent)
- *Why Nations Fail* — Acemoglu & Robinson (broader comparative argument about institutional quality and outcomes)
- *The Blundering Government* — Various (the academic political science literature on policy failure that King and Crewe draw from)

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

- **Blunders have a signature.** They are not random. When you see hubris + speed + inadequate piloting + ministerial churn + weak evaluation, the probability of disaster is high. The syndrome is diagnosable in advance.
- **Ministerial tenure is a risk variable.** A minister who will not be in post when outcomes arrive has structurally weakened incentives to design for long-term success. Tenure length matters for accountability.
- **The knowledge asymmetry with contractors is a structural problem.** Government departments negotiating with specialist contractors on complex, long-term contracts will routinely produce worse outcomes than those contractors expect. This is not fixable by individual competence — it requires structural changes to how government builds expertise.
- **Announcement and implementation are different skills, and we reward the wrong one.** The political system's incentive to announce produces a consistent bias toward speed over adequacy. Organisations that reward piloting and evaluation will produce better outcomes.
- **Feedback mechanisms must be designed, not assumed.** The absence of effective feedback between policy outcomes and policy design is not a natural state — it is a product of specific institutional choices. Parliamentary committees, policy evaluation units, whistleblower protections, and freedom of information are not nice-to-haves; they are the mechanisms that allow error correction.
- **Group-think in government is structural, not incidental.** It arises from career incentives, ministerial culture, and the absence of institutionalised adversarialism. Changing it requires changing incentive structures, not personality.
- **The doctrine of ministerial responsibility, as currently practised, is fiction.** Accountability that never attaches to anyone does not discipline behaviour. If you are designing accountability mechanisms, ensure they produce actual consequences.

## FURTHER READING

1. **The Welfare Trait** — Adam Perkins: A controversial but useful complement on how welfare reform assumptions have repeatedly failed contact with reality — extends the "cultural disconnect" argument.
2. **Why Government Fails So Often** — Peter Schuck: A US-focused counterpart to King and Crewe, with a broader comparative framework.
3. **Thinking in Systems** — Donella Meadows: Provides the systems-thinking vocabulary that explains *why* the feedback loops King and Crewe identify are so persistently broken.
