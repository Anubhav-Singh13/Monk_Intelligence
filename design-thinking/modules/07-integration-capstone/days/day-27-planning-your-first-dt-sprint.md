---

## Building the intuition

A 3-day mini-sprint is a compressed version of the full DT loop. It is not a Design Sprint (Knapp's 5-day format) — it is shorter and more flexible. It is not a workshop — it produces real deliverables that feed real product decisions. It is not a research project — it includes both research and concept development.

**What makes a mini-sprint work:**

1. **A committed team of 3–5 people** for all 3 days. The team must include someone who owns the product decision, someone with access to users, and at least one person who will build whatever the sprint validates.

2. **A specific problem to focus on** — not "let's explore the product direction" but "we don't know why users abandon during onboarding, and we need to know before planning Q3." The problem must be specific enough to produce a testable concept in three days.

3. **Real users to talk to and test with** — pre-recruited before Day 1. If you can't reach your actual users in three days, the sprint won't produce actionable learning. This is the most common planning failure.

4. **Protected time** — no other meetings for the sprint participants during the three days. One day of partial attention produces less than half a day of full attention.

## The formal picture

**3-Day DT Mini-Sprint — Full Schedule:**

---

### Day 1 — Understand and Define (7 hours)

| Time | Activity | Output |
|------|----------|--------|
| 9:00–9:30 | **Sprint kick-off:** Agree on the problem scope. What is the specific decision this sprint will inform? | Problem statement (1 sentence) |
| 9:30–11:00 | **Expert briefings:** Each team member shares what they already know — data, past research, customer quotes, relevant analytics. Use lightning demos: 3 min per person. | Shared knowledge baseline |
| 11:00–12:30 | **Empathy review / mini-interviews:** If you have existing user research, review it and extract the most important observations. If not: conduct 3 rapid 20-min interviews with users recruited in advance. | Raw observations on sticky notes |
| 12:30–13:30 | Lunch |  |
| 13:30–14:30 | **Empathy mapping:** Build one empathy map as a team from the morning's observations | Empathy map |
| 14:30–15:30 | **Insight synthesis:** Find 3 candidate insights using the Day 12 process. Apply the three-question test. Commit to one. | 1 validated insight |
| 15:30–16:30 | **POV + HMW:** Write the POV statement together. Generate 5–7 HMW variants. Select 2 to carry into Day 2. | POV statement + 2 HMW questions |
| 16:30–17:00 | **Day 1 retrospective:** What did we learn? What assumptions changed? Is the problem still the right problem? | Adjusted problem scope (if needed) |

---

### Day 2 — Ideate and Prototype (7 hours)

| Time | Activity | Output |
|------|----------|--------|
| 9:00–9:15 | **Day 1 recap:** Re-read the POV and both HMW questions aloud. | Shared alignment |
| 9:15–10:00 | **Individual ideation:** Silent sticky-note brainstorm on HMW 1 (20 min) + HMW 2 (20 min). | 40–60 ideas |
| 10:00–10:30 | **Share and build:** Place all notes on the wall; one combinatorial round. | 60–80 ideas |
| 10:30–11:00 | **Affinity mapping + dot voting:** Cluster; vote. | 3 concept sketches selected |
| 11:00–12:30 | **Concept development:** Each person sketches one concept in detail — 4-panel storyboard + description. | 3 concept descriptions |
| 12:30–13:30 | Lunch |  |
| 13:30–14:00 | **Concept selection:** Review 3 concepts; apply assumption mapping. Identify the riskiest assumption for each. Select 1 (or 2) to prototype. | 1–2 concepts to prototype |
| 14:00–16:30 | **Build the prototype:** Paper prototype (screen-based) or storyboard (service concept) or role-play script. Goal: something a real user can interact with tomorrow. | 1–2 testable prototypes |
| 16:30–17:00 | **Test prep:** Write the hypothesis, the user task, and the note-taking grid. Confirm user recruitment for Day 3. | Test plan ready |

---

### Day 3 — Test and Decide (5 hours)

| Time | Activity | Output |
|------|----------|--------|
| 9:00–9:30 | **Final prototype review:** Brief walk-through of the prototype and test protocol. | Team aligned on test plan |
| 9:30–12:30 | **User testing:** 5 sessions × 30 min each. One team member facilitates; another takes notes. Run sequentially. | Raw test notes (per session) |
| 12:30–13:15 | Lunch |  |
| 13:15–14:00 | **Synthesis:** Works/Stuck/Surprised sort. Find patterns. Write 3 synthesis statements. | 3 synthesis statements |
| 14:00–14:45 | **Decision:** Apply the Day 25 framework. Refine / Reframe / Proceed? Document the decision and the evidence behind it. | Sprint decision document |
| 14:45–15:30 | **Hand-off prep:** Write the enriched user story (Day 26 format). Name what was confirmed, what remains untested, and the recommended next step. | Sprint output document |
| 15:30–16:00 | **Sprint retrospective:** What would you do differently? What worked? What should the next sprint focus on? | Lessons learned |

---

**What you leave the sprint with:**

```
Sprint output document:
├── Problem statement (the specific decision the sprint informed)
├── Empathy map (one archetype)
├── Insight (validated, with traceability)
├── POV statement
├── HMW question(s)
├── Concept sketch (the tested concept)
├── Test hypothesis + protocol used
├── 3 synthesis statements from testing
├── Sprint decision: Refine / Reframe / Proceed
└── Enriched user story (ready for backlog)
```

This document is the hand-off artifact from Day 26. It replaces 6 weeks of assumption-based planning with 3 days of evidence-based direction.

---

## Where it breaks / what it is not

**The hardest part is user recruitment, not facilitation.** Teams that run the sprint brilliantly but have to test with internal employees or self-selected enthusiasts get misleading results. Recruit real users before Day 1. If you can't, the sprint should be postponed — not run with the wrong participants.

**Day 2's prototype does not need to be beautiful.** The most common sprint Day 2 failure: the team spends 4 hours making a polished Figma prototype when a 45-minute paper prototype would serve the same purpose. Default to paper. If someone insists on Figma, ask: "What question would the Figma answer that paper can't?" Usually there is no good answer.

**Three days is enough to learn — not to build.** The sprint output is a *decision* and an *enriched user story* — not a shipped feature, not a backlog of 40 tickets, not a product roadmap. Teams that treat the sprint output as a spec and hand it directly to engineering without a planning conversation will produce the wrong feature from correct research.

**Exercise 2 — Direct application.** You are planning a mini-sprint for your team to investigate why enterprise customers churn in the first 60 days. Adapt the Day 1 schedule: what specific activities would you run in the 9:30–11:00 "Expert briefings" slot — given that your team has 6 months of customer success data and 3 previous exit interview transcripts?

<details>
<summary>A strong adaptation</summary>

**9:30–10:00 — Data lightning demos (30 min):**
Each person presents one thing they know from existing sources in 5 min max:
- PM: churn timeline analysis — when in the 60-day window do churns happen? (pull from product analytics)
- CS lead: top 3 reasons cited in exit interviews (review transcripts)
- Sales: profile of churned customers vs. retained — any segment patterns?
- Engineer: feature usage data for churned cohort — what did they use/not use?
- PM again: NPS or CSAT scores at 30-day checkpoint

**10:00–11:00 — Transcript analysis (60 min):**
Read the 3 exit interview transcripts as a team. Each person reads silently and writes the most important observation on a sticky note — one per transcript, one per person. Then: place notes on a wall, cluster by theme. This replaces "conducting new interviews" on Day 1 when strong existing data exists — a legitimate shortcut that preserves research rigor without duplicating work already done.

**Key adaptation principle:** Day 1's goal is "shared understanding of what we already know + what we still don't know." If existing data is rich, lean on it. The empathy interviews (11:00–12:30 slot) become more targeted — you know the general pattern, so you can go deeper on the specific moments that existing data can't explain.
</details>

---

**Exercise 3 — Stretch.** A colleague who runs a 3-person startup says: "This schedule is designed for big teams — I can't take 3 full days off delivery." Propose a compressed, realistic adaptation for a solo founder who can commit only 4 total hours across one week (not consecutive days) and has access to 3 existing customers for a 20-minute call each.

<details>
<summary>A compressed adaptation</summary>

**Hour 1 (Monday — Define):** Review existing customer messages and support tickets for 20 minutes. Write 3 observations on sticky notes. Spend 40 minutes writing: one insight, one POV statement, two HMW questions. This replaces the full Day 1 sprint — it works because a solo founder has deep existing context that a large team's Day 1 is designed to surface.

**Hours 2–3 (Tuesday/Wednesday — Prototype):** Sketch one concept (paper prototype or storyboard) in 30 minutes — no more. Build a 5-question test script: one task, 4 debrief questions. Set up 3 × 20-min customer calls for Thursday/Friday.

**Hour 4 (Friday — Test + decide):** Run 3 × 20-minute calls using the test protocol. After the last call, spend 15 minutes writing 3 synthesis statements from memory (while observations are fresh). Spend the final 15 minutes applying the Day 25 decision: Refine, Reframe, or Proceed?

**Total:** 4 hours. Output: one tested concept, 3 synthesis statements, one decision, one enriched user story. The schedule is leaner; the rigor is the same. What is sacrificed is team diversity (one perspective, not five) — which is a real limitation, not just a scheduling trade-off.
</details>
