# Day 28 — Capstone: Run Your First DT Cycle

> **Today's one idea:** Design Thinking is not something you understand — it is something you do. This page gives you one real problem, one real day, and everything you need to run the full cycle without a script.
> **Reading time:** ~30 min to read. 3–4 hours to complete (or across 3 days if you need field access for empathy). Plan your schedule before starting.
> **Prereqs:** Days 1–27.
> **Before you start:** One final retrieval challenge. Without looking: state the five phases of the DT loop, and for each phase name the single most important output artifact. Do this now — before reading another word.

---

## What this capstone is

This is not an exercise. It is not a simulation. It is not a test with a right answer.

This capstone is a directive: **choose a real problem from your current work, run a full DT cycle on it, and produce eight specific artifacts**. The problem must be one where you have genuine access to real users — not hypothetical users, not colleagues pretending to be users, not internal stakeholders.

You have 27 days of preparation. You know what each artifact is, how to produce it, and what makes it good versus weak. The only remaining question is whether you can produce them without being told step-by-step what to do.

That question is the capstone.

---

## Choosing your problem

A good capstone problem has three properties:

**1. Real user uncertainty.** You don't fully know why users behave the way they do, what they actually need, or whether your current solution is addressing the right problem. If you already know the answer, pick a different problem.

**2. Real access.** You can talk to 2–3 real users (or observe 1–2) within the time you've set aside. "Users" means the people who actually do the thing you're designing for — not your manager, not your team, not your most engaged power user who has internalized your product's assumptions.

**3. Real stakes.** The problem is connected to a real decision your team will make in the next 4–8 weeks. The artifacts you produce should be useful, not academic.

**Examples of good capstone problems:**
- "We don't know why new enterprise users don't complete onboarding past day 3"
- "Our internal tool has low adoption — we've assumed it's a feature problem, but we don't actually know"
- "The client brief process takes 3x longer than it should and we don't know where the friction lives"
- "We're building a new feature for field nurses — we've written the user stories but haven't talked to a single nurse"

**Examples of problems to avoid:**
- "Our checkout conversion is low" — you need analytics, not DT
- "We need a better internal wiki" — too vague, no specific user behavior to investigate
- "I want to redesign the onboarding" — this is a solution, not a problem; back up and find the actual need

Write your problem statement before proceeding. It should be one sentence: *"We don't know [specific thing] about [specific user group] and we need to know it before [specific decision]."*

## The eight artifacts

Your capstone is complete when you have produced all eight of the following artifacts. Each one connects directly to a course day — use those pages as reference if needed.

---

### Artifact 1 — Empathy notes (Day 7 / 8)

Conduct 2–3 real empathy interviews or 1–2 field observations. Use the four-phase interview structure from Day 7. Take raw behavioral notes — what people said and did, not your interpretations.

**Minimum standard:** At least 2 sessions. Notes must distinguish what was observed (behavior) from what was inferred (interpretation). Use the AEIOU framework from Day 8 if conducting observation.

**Done when:** You have at least 15 behavioral observations across your sessions, documented in writing.

---

### Artifact 2 — Empathy map (Day 9)

Synthesize your empathy notes into a single empathy map for your primary user archetype. Use the Says / Does / Thinks / Feels + Pains / Gains structure.

**Minimum standard:** All six quadrants filled. At least one observation in each. The Says/Does gap explicitly noted if one exists.

**Done when:** You can look at the map and immediately articulate one tension or contradiction that wasn't obvious before you built it.

---

### Artifact 3 — Insight + POV statement (Days 12–13)

From the empathy map, extract one validated insight. Apply the three-question insight test. Then write your POV statement in the d.school format: *[User] needs [need] because [insight].*

**Minimum standard:** The insight must be surprising (it reframes something you assumed), specific (about a particular behavior), and actionable (it points toward a design direction). The POV must pass the "does it generate 5 different HMW questions?" test.

**Done when:** You have one insight with documented reasoning (why it passes the three-question test) and one complete POV statement.

---

### Artifact 4 — How Might We question (Day 14)

Generate at least 7 HMW variants from your POV statement, using the five reframing moves from Day 14. Select the one that opens the most generative design space — right-sized (generates 20+ distinct ideas, not just 5 variations of the same idea).

**Minimum standard:** At least 7 HMW variants in writing. Selected HMW circled with a one-sentence justification for why it's the right aperture.

**Done when:** You can generate 10 different ideas from your selected HMW without straining.

---

### Artifact 5 — Concept sketch (Days 16–18)

Ideate on your HMW. Run a silent individual brainstorm first (Day 16 structure). Then apply at least one secondary technique from Day 17 (SCAMPER, Worst Possible Idea, or Analogous Inspiration). Use dot voting (Day 18) to select your top concept — even if you're working alone, force a dot vote (give yourself 5 dots; commit to where they fall).

Develop your selected concept into a 4-panel storyboard or written concept description with a rough sketch.

**Minimum standard:** At least 20 ideas generated in writing. One concept developed into a storyboard or written description with a sketch. Selected concept justified against your HMW.

**Done when:** You can show the concept sketch to someone unfamiliar with your project and they understand what it does and who it's for without additional explanation.

---

### Artifact 6 — Paper prototype (Days 19–20)

Build a paper prototype or low-fidelity artifact of your selected concept. This must be something a real user can interact with — they should be able to point, tap, or move through it as if it were a real product.

If your concept is a digital product: paper UI screens (at least 3 states/screens), with annotations. If your concept is a service or process: a role-play script or a storyboard the user walks through with you. If your concept is a physical object: a physical mock-up from available materials.

**Minimum standard:** The prototype must test one specific hypothesis (Day 19 format: "We believe [concept detail] will [user behavior], because [Day 12 insight]"). It must be faster to build than to argue about — if you spent more than 60 minutes on it, it's too polished.

**Done when:** You have a testable artifact and a written hypothesis.

---

### Artifact 7 — Test observations (Days 23–24)

Run at least 2 test sessions (3 preferred) using your paper prototype. Use the Day 23 session structure: task → think-aloud → debrief questions. Take behavioral notes using the "facts before interpretations" rule.

After all sessions: sort observations into Works / Stuck / Surprised (Day 24). Write 3 synthesis statements using the format: *"Users consistently [behavior] when [trigger], which suggests [implication]."*

**Minimum standard:** At least 2 sessions. At least 10 behavioral observations per session. 3 synthesis statements written. Synthesis statements distinguish between a Refine signal and a Reframe signal.

**Done when:** You can read your three synthesis statements and immediately say whether the evidence points to Refine, Reframe, or Proceed.

---

### Artifact 8 — Iteration decision + one-page retrospective (Day 25)

Apply the Day 25 three-question test to your synthesis statements. State your decision: Refine, Reframe, or Proceed. Write one paragraph (not more) justifying the decision with specific reference to your synthesis statements.

Then write your retrospective: a single page (or ~300 words) covering:

1. **What surprised you most** in the empathy phase — the observation you did not expect
2. **Where synthesis was hardest** — which step in the data → insight → POV → HMW chain was the most difficult, and what made it so
3. **What you would do differently** in the next DT cycle
4. **What the next step is** — if Proceed: what goes into the backlog; if Refine: what specifically changes; if Reframe: which phase you return to and why

**Minimum standard:** Decision is stated with one sentence of evidence. Retrospective addresses all four points above. The retrospective is honest — not a summary of "what DT is" but a specific reflection on *this* cycle with *this* problem.

**Done when:** Someone who doesn't know your problem can read the retrospective and understand what you learned, what you decided, and why.

---

## Done = all eight

You are done when all eight artifacts exist in writing. They do not need to be in a presentation deck. They do not need to look beautiful. They need to be honest, specific, and complete.

Print them, paste them into a document, or put them in a folder in this wiki. It does not matter. What matters is that they exist — that the full DT loop ran on a real problem, and that you can point to the moment where research changed what you thought you knew.

---

## A note on the synthesis artifacts

If you hit the capstone and find that Artifacts 3 and 4 (insight, POV, HMW) feel difficult or uncertain — go back to Day 15 (Drill: Synthesis) before continuing. The drill exists for this moment.

The synthesis chain (data → insight → POV → HMW) is the rate-limiting step of the entire DT practice. It was identified as such on Day 1, drilled on Day 15, and called back in every subsequent module. If the capstone reveals a gap in the chain, that is the most valuable feedback the course can give you — it tells you exactly where to invest the next 10 hours of deliberate practice.

## Suggested readings for today

**Before you start the capstone:**
Knapp, Jake, *Sprint* (Simon & Schuster, 2016), "After the Sprint" (pp. 253–270). Knapp's description of what happens after the sprint output document lands with the team — how to present findings, how to handle "what do we build next," and how to make the evidence actionable in a real organization. Reading time: ~20 minutes. Do this before your first empathy session.

**Free video — watch before Artifact 7:**
NNgroup, *"How to Conduct a Usability Test"* — full walkthrough. Search YouTube: `NNgroup how to conduct a usability test`. ~12 min. The most rigorous free treatment of the Day 23 session protocol, with facilitation examples. Watch this before running your test sessions if you haven't conducted user research before.

**Free video — the full picture:**
IDEO, *"Design Thinking"* — full overview. Search YouTube: `IDEO design thinking process full`. ~5 min. After completing the capstone, watch this as a retrospective anchor — compare what IDEO describes to what you experienced. The gaps between the description and your experience are the most valuable data points for your continued DT practice.

**After the capstone:**
Brown, Tim. *Change by Design.* HarperBusiness, 2009. Chapter 8 "The Design of Design Thinking Organizations." If you are thinking about how to bring DT practice into your team or organization — not just use it yourself — this chapter is the most actionable treatment of that question. It directly answers what you'll be asked when you present your capstone artifacts to your stakeholders.

## Retrospective: the course itself

You have spent 28 days with a single idea: that the hardest part of making anything is not building it — it is finding the right problem to build for.

The five phases, the synthesis chain, the prototyping discipline, the testing structure — they are all in service of one thing: the moment when you can say, with evidence, *"I know who I'm designing for, what they actually need, and why my current concept is likely to serve that need."* That is the practitioner outcome this course was built for.

The next DT cycle will be faster. The one after that, faster still. The chain — data → insight → POV → HMW — will eventually become a habit of mind, not a procedure. That is when DT stops being a methodology and starts being how you think.

Good luck.
