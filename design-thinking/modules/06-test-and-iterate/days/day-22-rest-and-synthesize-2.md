<details>
<summary>Compare to these</summary>

1. **What to Prototype (Day 21):** Prototype the riskiest assumption first — the one that, if false, makes everything else irrelevant. Use an assumption map (importance vs. evidence) to identify it.
2. **From Data to Insight (Day 12):** An insight is a surprising, reframing, actionable pattern derived from empathy data — not a data point. It requires asking "why" until the root cause changes how you see the problem.
3. **Selecting and Clustering Ideas (Day 18):** Affinity mapping groups ideas by theme without verbal influence; dot voting selects the 2–3 most worth prototyping democratically and silently. Both steps happen before discussion.
4. **What Is a Prototype? (Day 19):** A prototype is disposable, tests one specific hypothesis, and should be faster to build than to argue about. Use the lowest fidelity that answers your current question.
5. **HMW Questions (Day 14):** A HMW bridges a locked problem definition to open ideation — wide enough for 20 meaningfully different ideas, narrow enough that all are grounded in the user need. Three words: How (solvable), Might (open), We (collaborative).
6. **Brainstorming Done Right (Day 16):** Two conditions: quantity before quality (volume over evaluation) and deferred judgment (no evaluation during generation). Silent sticky-note generation prevents anchoring and social filtering.
7. **Low-Fidelity Prototyping (Day 20):** Paper prototypes, storyboards, and role-plays test assumptions 10× faster than code, surface the same usability issues, and cost nothing to discard.
8. **POV Statement (Day 13):** [User description] needs [need — a verb phrase, not a solution] because [surprising insight]. All three components must trace to real research. The POV is the team's committed design brief.
9. **Ideation Beyond Brainstorming (Day 17):** SCAMPER transforms existing ideas through seven operators; Worst Possible Idea relieves social pressure by inverting the goal; Analogous Inspiration borrows principles (not solutions) from other domains.
10. **Drill Day (Day 15):** The synthesis drill confirmed your ability (or gap) at the full chain: raw data → pattern → insight → POV → HMW. Whichever exercise felt hardest is your personal bottleneck.
</details>

```
  Rule: Use lowest _______________ that answers current question
```

<details>
<summary>Filled blanks</summary>

**DEFINE:**
- Blank A: Empathy data — observations, interview notes, empathy maps, journey maps
- Pattern; "why"; POV; HMW

**IDEATE:**
- Blank B: Silent sticky-note brainstorm
- Blank C: SCAMPER, Worst Possible Idea (or Analogous Inspiration)
- Blank D: Affinity mapping → dot voting
- Output: 2 to 3 concept sketches

**PROTOTYPE:**
- Importance vs. Evidence (assumption map)
- Blank E: Paper prototype, storyboard, role-play
- Hypothesis
- Fidelity
</details>

**Questions to answer without looking:**

1. What makes a POV statement's "because" clause an insight vs. an observation?
2. What is the silent rule that applies during both affinity mapping and dot voting — and why?
3. What is the difference between a storyboard and a paper prototype — what does each one test?
4. A concept has five assumptions. You have one week. How do you decide which to test?
5. Why does deferred judgment in brainstorming and silence in convergence solve the same underlying problem?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. The "because" clause is an insight when it is surprising (couldn't have been written before research), reframes the problem (shifts attention to a root cause, not a symptom), and is actionable (suggests a design direction). A pure observation describes what happened; an insight explains *why* in a way that changes what you'd design.

2. **No discussion during either phase.** Reason: verbal interaction during both diverge and converge activates the same failure modes — anchoring to the first idea spoken and social filtering toward the most confidently expressed view. Silence removes both.

3. **Storyboard** — tests whether the *narrative* of the user's experience with the concept is plausible end-to-end (before/during/after panels). **Paper prototype** — tests whether users can *navigate* a screen-based interface and whether the flow logic makes sense. One tests story; one tests usability.

4. Map all five on importance (if wrong, concept fails?) vs. evidence (how confident are you?). Test the top-left quadrant first: highest importance, lowest evidence. That is the assumption whose falsity would make all other work irrelevant.

5. Both problems are caused by the same cognitive failure mode: social influence on creative or evaluative judgment. In brainstorming, the first idea spoken anchors everyone's thinking. In convergence, the most confident speaker dominates the selection. Silence removes the social channel through which both failures propagate — in both cases, visual/written artifacts replace verbal influence.
</details>

## Step 4 — Quiz (5 min)

Answer before looking:

1. You have 80 sticky notes on the wall from a brainstorm. A senior stakeholder walks in and says "let's just vote on the top 10." What is missing from this process — and what specifically goes wrong if you skip it?

2. Your POV says: "A charge nurse needs to trust the medication record because the 3-minute lag creates a hidden safety window." Your team generates the HMW: "How might we make the medication record update faster?" Is this HMW well-calibrated? If not, what is wrong?

3. You run a paper prototype session. Users complete all tasks successfully. A teammate says: "Great — let's build it." What does the DT practitioner say next?

4. Analogous Inspiration requires "abstracting the problem above the domain level." Why? What goes wrong if you skip the abstraction step?

5. **Cross-concept:** Your prototype test reveals that users can navigate the interface (usability confirmed) but don't understand *why they would use it* (desirability uncertain). Using concepts from Days 12, 13, 19, and 21, explain what this finding tells you about the quality of your POV statement — and what your next step is.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. **What's missing:** Affinity mapping — the step that groups ideas into themes before voting. **What goes wrong:** without clustering, dot votes are cast on 80 individual fragments rather than on 5–8 thematic directions. The result is that similar ideas split their votes, outlier ideas get zero attention, and the team has no shared map of the solution space. They also skip the "low-vote long shot" — an unconventional idea that challenges the main concept's assumptions.

2. **Not well-calibrated — too narrow.** "Make the record update faster" is already a solution direction (faster updates = engineering problem). It has closed the design space before ideation starts. The POV's insight ("trust deficit + hidden safety window") points to a much wider space — how might we help nurses *trust* the record's currency, which could include faster updates but also visible timestamps, dual-confirmation systems, or verbal read-back protocols. A better HMW: "How might we help charge nurses feel confident that the medication record reflects the patient's current state?"

3. **What the practitioner says:** "What assumption did we just confirm — and what's the *next* riskiest assumption we haven't tested yet?" Successful task completion in a paper prototype confirms *usability* of the current flow. It does not confirm *desirability* (will users want this?), *feasibility* (can it be built?), or whether the concept addresses the right problem. The assumption map should be rebuilt with usability now marked as confirmed, and the next prototype should target the next highest-risk assumption.

4. **Why abstract:** Without abstraction, you look for solutions in your own industry that look like your problem — you find competitors and copy features (mimicry). Abstraction ("the problem is building trust in a safety-critical real-time information system") opens the search space to aviation, banking, air traffic control, nuclear power — domains that have solved harder versions of the same underlying problem. If you skip abstraction, you borrow solutions instead of principles, and solutions rarely transfer without modification.

5. **What the finding tells you about the POV:** The desirability gap — users understand *how* to use the product but not *why they would* — is almost always a symptom of a POV that didn't fully capture the latent need. The "need" clause in your POV may have been too solution-shaped (it described a feature, not a genuine human need), or the "because" clause may not have been a real insight — it didn't reframe the problem compellingly enough to make the *why* obvious to users when they encounter the prototype. **Next step:** Return to Define. Rewrite the POV's need clause as a genuine behavioral need ("needs to feel X" or "needs to be able to do Y"), verify the insight traces to real observation, then regenerate the HMW. The prototype that just failed was testing the right usability — but the wrong concept direction.
</details>
