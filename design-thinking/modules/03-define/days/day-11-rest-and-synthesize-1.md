<details>
<summary>Compare to these</summary>

1. **Empathy Map (Day 9):** An empathy map synthesizes what a user says, does, thinks, and feels into one shared artifact — the most value comes from the gap between Says and Does.
2. **What DT Is (Day 1):** Design Thinking is a problem-finding discipline before it is a problem-solving one — it treats the problem statement as the first thing to interrogate.
3. **Observation in the Field (Day 8):** What people do vs. what they say reveals hidden needs; observation captures workarounds, body language, and environmental context that interviews miss.
4. **Wicked Problems (Day 5):** DT is most valuable when the question itself is wrong — when defining the problem IS the real work, because no definitive formulation exists.
5. **The Human at the Center (Day 2):** You design for behavior and context, not for stated preferences — users are experts in their experience, not in their needs.
6. **Journey Mapping (Day 10):** A journey map shows where pain and delight cluster across a user's experience over time; the experience curve makes the highest-priority pain point visible.
7. **Agile Brain Upgraded (Day 4):** DT finds the right problem; Agile builds it right — DT is upstream of Agile, not a replacement for it.
8. **Empathy vs. Sympathy (Day 6):** Empathy in DT means observing latent needs from the user's actual context — three reasons users can't fully self-report: adaptation, articulation gap, optimism bias.
9. **Five Phases as One Loop (Day 3):** DT is a diverge–converge rhythm repeated; the backward arrows (Test → Define, Test → Empathize) are not failures but the mechanism by which DT works.
10. **Research Interview (Day 7):** A DT interview asks about specific past incidents, not general habits or opinions — story questions are at the top of the usefulness hierarchy.
</details>

Fill in the blanks from memory. Every blank is labelled with what it is asking for.

```
  Output: _______________

        ↓ (converge)

  Primary question: [blank C — what the phase is asking]
  Output: _______________  +  _______________ question

        ↓ (diverge)

  Output: Many ideas → select _____ to prototype

        ↓ (converge)

  Output: A _______________ made tangible (for learning, not impressing)

        ↓ (diverge → converge)

Phase 5: _______________
  Three decisions after testing: _______________, _______________, _______________

        ↑ backward arrows possible to any earlier phase
```

<details>
<summary>Filled-in flow</summary>

**Phase 1: Empathize**
- Primary question: Who are we designing for and what do they actually experience?
- Output: Stories, observations, quotes, behaviors
- Key tools: Research interview + field observation (→ empathy map + journey map)

**Phase 2: Define**
- Primary question: What is the real problem, stated as a human need?
- Output: POV statement + HMW question

**Phase 3: Ideate**
- Output: Many ideas → select 2–3 to prototype

**Phase 4: Prototype**
- Output: A rough artifact made tangible (for learning, not impressing)

**Phase 5: Test**
- Three decisions: refine (solution was close), reframe (problem was wrong), proceed (concept validated)
</details>

**Questions to answer without looking:**

1. What is the output of the Empathize phase that carries into Define?
2. Why does the DT loop have backward arrows — what do they represent?
3. What is the difference between an empathy map and a journey map? (One sentence each.)
4. What does "tame problem" vs. "wicked problem" determine about whether to use DT?
5. At what stage does "what users say" most strongly diverge from "what users do" — and what tool surfaces that gap?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Rich observations — stories, behavioral notes, quotes, and the two synthesized artifacts (empathy map and journey map) — carry into Define. Raw data carries forward; the synthesis tools organize it.
2. Backward arrows represent learning that invalidates the current phase's output. They are not failures — they are the mechanism. If testing shows the POV was wrong, you go back to Define. If it shows you misunderstood the user entirely, you go back to Empathize.
3. **Empathy map:** a static portrait of one user at one moment — who they are, what they say/do/think/feel. **Journey map:** a dynamic story of one user across time — what happens to them stage by stage, where pain clusters.
4. Tame problems (defined formulation, known solution space, measurable progress) don't need DT — use standard engineering. Wicked problems (contested formulation, no obvious right answer, defining the problem IS the work) are where DT is indispensable.
5. The empathy map's Says/Does quadrant pair surfaces the gap most explicitly. Observation in the field (Day 8) is the method that *collects* the Does data that the Says/Does contradiction requires.
</details>

## Step 3 — Run / apply it (10 min)

Choose a real problem from your work — one you've been near recently. From memory (no notes), do the following:

- [ ] Name the user archetype affected by this problem
- [ ] Write one thing they would **Say** about the problem
- [ ] Write one thing they would **Do** that might contradict that (observed or inferred)
- [ ] Write the resulting gap as one sentence: "They say X but they do Y, which suggests..."
- [ ] Decide: is this a tame or wicked problem? State why in one sentence
- [ ] Sketch (in words) a 5-stage journey map: name the stages and mark which one has the deepest emotional trough

If any step goes blank, that is your diagnostic. Note it. That is the exact topic to re-read before Day 12.

## Step 4 — Quiz (5 min)

> **Why the questions jump around:** Questions in day-order let you answer by position. The questions below deliberately cross concept boundaries. Answer each cold.

1. The journey map's experience curve shows a deep trough at the "Onboarding" stage. What does the DT practitioner do *before* writing a HMW question for that pain point?

2. A user in an interview says: "The process is fine." An observer watches the same user spend 8 minutes on a manual workaround before every transaction. Which of Norman's two gulfs does this most likely reflect, and what DT tool surfaces the contradiction?

3. Day 4 said DT is "upstream" of Agile. Where specifically does a journey map's "Opportunities" row connect to Agile backlog creation?

4. You have empathy data from 5 interviews but no field observation sessions. Which of the three reasons from Day 6 (adaptation, articulation gap, optimism bias) does this gap most threaten to leave unaddressed — and why?

5. **Cross-concept:** A product team has a detailed journey map showing a pain spike at sign-up. They write a HMW question: "How might we make sign-up faster?" A DT practitioner reviews it and says: "This isn't ready for Ideate yet." Using concepts from at least three different days (1–10), explain why.

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. Before writing a HMW question, the practitioner must first synthesize an **insight** — a surprising pattern that reframes what the pain point is actually about. The journey map identifies *where* the pain is; the insight identifies *why* — and the why shapes what kind of HMW question is worth asking. (Day 12 teaches this explicitly.)

2. Most likely the **Gulf of Evaluation** — the user believes the process is working (their evaluation of the system state is disconnected from reality), which is why they say "it's fine." The **empathy map's Says/Does gap** surfaces the contradiction — the Says quadrant has "it's fine" and the Does quadrant has the 8-minute workaround. (Days 2, 9.)

3. The journey map's Opportunities row generates seed HMW questions. Those HMW questions, once developed in the Define phase, become the input to Ideate — where concepts are generated. Those validated concepts then feed **Sprint 0 / backlog seeding** (Day 4's integration diagram) as user stories with verified need context behind them.

4. **Adaptation.** Field observation is the primary tool for catching behaviors users have adapted to so thoroughly they no longer notice or report them. Interviews (even very good ones) will mostly surface articulated frustrations and self-reported behavior — they are weaker at revealing automatic workarounds the user doesn't consciously recognize as problems. The articulation gap is also a risk, but adaptation specifically requires observational access to the behavior in context.

5. **Cross-concept answer:** (1) The journey map shows *where* the pain is (Day 10) but not *why* — the team has skipped the synthesis step of forming an insight (Day 12). "Sign-up is slow" is a data point, not an insight. (2) The HMW question was generated from a surface symptom, not from a POV statement grounded in a specific user and a specific latent need (Day 13). Without a POV, the HMW has no anchor — it could generate solutions for entirely the wrong user. (3) The team has assumed the problem is execution speed when observation might reveal the real issue is trust, confusion about what's being asked for, or fear about data privacy (Days 6–8). The "how might we make it faster" framing is an opinion about the solution, not a synthesis of what users actually experience.
</details>
