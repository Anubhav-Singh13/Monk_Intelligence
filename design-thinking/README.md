# Design Thinking for Product Development

> **The promise:** By Day 28, you will be able to plan and facilitate a Design Thinking cycle on a real product problem — moving from raw user observation to a tested, iterated concept — and integrate that cycle into your existing Agile workflow.

---

## Who this course is for

This course is for practitioners with an Agile background who want to add a rigorous, human-centered problem-finding discipline to their product development process. No prior UX or design experience is assumed. The course targets **L1 — Practitioner** depth: you will learn to use Design Thinking well, reason about when to apply each phase, and facilitate sessions with a real team. You will not become a design researcher or DT coach — that is L2 territory.

If you already run user research at a professional level, or if you want to train others in DT methodology, this course will feel too introductory. If you have never thought about *finding* the right problem (only *solving* a given one), this course is exactly right.

---

## The arc at a glance

On Day 1, you arrive knowing Agile but not yet knowing why Design Thinking exists alongside it. You can build things right; you don't yet have a discipline for finding the right thing to build. The first five days install that mental model — DT as a problem-finding loop, not a solution-generating one — and resolve the Agile/DT tension before it becomes an obstacle. By Day 10, you have a full empathy toolkit and two synthesis artifacts (empathy map, journey map) in hand, even if synthesis still feels slippery.

The middle arc (Days 12–21) is where the real work happens. Synthesis — moving from raw interview data to a sharp insight, a Point of View statement, and a "How Might We" question — is the hardest sub-skill at L1, and the course leans into it hard: a dedicated drill day, two retrieval checkpoints, and callbacks woven into the Test phase (Day 24) that show synthesis is not a Define-phase skill — it is the skill you use every time you make sense of observations. By Day 28, you don't just know the five phases. You can plan a 3-day mini-sprint, facilitate each phase, and make the judgment calls (when to pivot, what to prototype, which HMW to carry forward) that separate a practitioner from someone who has merely read about DT.

## How to use this course

- **Rhythm:** One page per day, 30–45 min of focused reading. Do not read ahead.
- **Exercises:** Non-negotiable at L1. The exercises are where the learning actually happens — reading without doing is tourism. Exercise 1 on every page must be done with the page closed.
- **Bibliography:** Check `bibliography.md` when a day's "Suggested readings" section points you there. Don't browse it proactively — use it as a reference.
- **Rest & Synthesize days (Days 11 and 22):** These are not days off. They are the hardest cognitive work in the course: retrieval from memory, cold, before any re-reading. Treat them as exams you give yourself.
- **Drill day (Day 15):** This is a gym session, not a lecture. Expect discomfort. The discomfort is the signal it is working.
- **If life intervenes:** Missing one day is fine — pick up where you left off. Missing a week: re-read the last two pages before continuing, then proceed. Do not restart.

## The capstone

On Day 28, you will choose a real problem from your current work — a feature with unclear user need, a process that keeps breaking, a product decision you're uncertain about — and run a compressed DT cycle: empathize (30 min of real or recalled observation), define (POV + HMW), ideate (20-idea diverge, 3-idea converge), prototype (one paper or wireframe artifact), and test (one real or simulated user session). You will document each artifact and write a one-page retrospective: what you learned that surprised you, and what you would do differently in the next iteration. "Done" means you have all five artifacts plus the retrospective, and you could walk a colleague through your reasoning at each phase transition.
