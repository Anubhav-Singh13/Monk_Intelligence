# Glossary — Design Thinking for Product Development

Alphabetical. Each term includes a plain-English definition, a formal definition, and the day it was first introduced.

---

**Affinity Mapping**
- *Plain English:* Grouping sticky notes (ideas, observations, quotes) by theme until patterns emerge.
- *Formal:* A bottom-up clustering method for organizing qualitative data into thematic groups without imposing a prior taxonomy.
- *Introduced:* Day 18

**Assumption**
- *Plain English:* Something you believe to be true about your user or solution that you haven't yet verified.
- *Formal:* An untested belief embedded in a design concept that, if false, would invalidate the concept.
- *Introduced:* Day 19

**Convergent Thinking**
- *Plain English:* Narrowing down — moving from many options to the best one.
- *Formal:* A mode of reasoning that applies constraints and criteria to reduce a solution space to a single answer or small set.
- *Introduced:* Day 3

**Design Sprint**
- *Plain English:* A 5-day structured protocol for running a compressed Design Thinking cycle on a product problem.
- *Formal:* A time-boxed product development framework (Knapp et al., 2016) that sequences problem framing, sketching, decision-making, prototyping, and user testing within a single week.
- *Introduced:* Day 27

**Design Thinking (DT)**
- *Plain English:* A way of approaching problems that starts with deeply understanding people, then defining the real problem, then generating and testing solutions.
- *Formal:* A human-centered, iterative problem-solving methodology characterized by empathetic inquiry, abductive reasoning, and experimental prototyping (Brown, 2008).
- *Introduced:* Day 1

**Divergent Thinking**
- *Plain English:* Opening up — generating as many possibilities as you can before evaluating any.
- *Formal:* A mode of reasoning that expands a solution space by suspending judgment and producing a high volume of varied options.
- *Introduced:* Day 3

**Dot Voting**
- *Plain English:* Each team member gets a fixed number of sticker dots and votes for the ideas they like best — the dots make priorities visible instantly.
- *Formal:* A multi-voting method for democratically prioritizing a list of options by distributing a finite number of votes across participants.
- *Introduced:* Day 18

**Empathy Map**
- *Plain English:* A one-page template that organizes what you observed about a user into four quadrants: what they say, do, think, and feel.
- *Formal:* A collaborative synthesis tool that captures and externalizes the experiential dimensions of a user persona derived from qualitative research.
- *Introduced:* Day 9

**HMW (How Might We)**
- *Plain English:* A question that reframes a problem as an open design opportunity — e.g., "How might we make it easier for nurses to track medication schedules?"
- *Formal:* A problem reframing construct that transforms a defined user need or insight into an actionable ideation prompt, balancing openness (might) with direction (we, how).
- *Introduced:* Day 14

**Insight**
- *Plain English:* A surprising realization from your research that changes how you see the problem — not just a data point, but a pattern that reframes everything.
- *Formal:* A synthesized, non-obvious finding derived from qualitative user research that reveals an underlying motivation, tension, or latent need not visible in surface-level data.
- *Introduced:* Day 12

**Journey Map**
- *Plain English:* A visual timeline of a user's experience with a product or service, showing their actions, emotions, and pain points at each step.
- *Formal:* A customer experience visualization tool that maps a user's interactions, thoughts, and emotional states across a defined scenario over time.
- *Introduced:* Day 10

**Latent Need**
- *Plain English:* Something a user wants or needs but cannot tell you directly — because they don't realize it, or because they've adapted to the problem.
- *Formal:* An unarticulated user requirement that underlies stated preferences and is discoverable only through behavioral observation or contextual inquiry.
- *Introduced:* Day 6

**Point of View (POV) Statement**
- *Plain English:* A one-sentence design brief that names the user, their need, and the surprising insight — the team's agreed-upon answer to "who are we designing for and why?"
- *Formal:* A human-centered problem statement of the form "[User] needs [need] because [surprising insight]" that anchors ideation to a specific, researched human context.
- *Introduced:* Day 13

**Prototype**
- *Plain English:* A rough, quick, physical or visual representation of an idea — built to answer a question, not to be finished.
- *Formal:* A low-cost, rapidly produced artifact representing one or more aspects of a design concept, intended to elicit user feedback and test specific assumptions.
- *Introduced:* Day 19

**SCAMPER**
- *Plain English:* A checklist of creative prompts — Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse — used to push ideas in new directions.
- *Formal:* A structured ideation technique that applies seven transformation operators to an existing concept to generate novel variants.
- *Introduced:* Day 17

**Synthesis**
- *Plain English:* The act of turning a pile of raw observations and quotes into a small number of sharp, meaningful statements that reveal what's really going on.
- *Formal:* The analytical process of transforming qualitative data (observations, interview transcripts, field notes) into insight statements, personas, or problem framings through pattern recognition and abductive reasoning.
- *Introduced:* Day 9 (introduced as a concept); Day 12 (treated formally as the rate-limiting sub-skill)

**User Testing**
- *Plain English:* Watching real people try to use your prototype, and listening carefully to what confuses or delights them.
- *Formal:* A formative evaluation method in which representative users interact with a prototype under observation to surface usability issues and validate or invalidate design assumptions.
- *Introduced:* Day 23

**Wicked Problem**
- *Plain English:* A problem so entangled with other problems that solving it changes what the problem is — there is no definitive formulation, and no clear right or wrong solution.
- *Formal:* A problem class (Rittel & Webber, 1973) characterized by incomplete, contradictory, and changing requirements, where solving one aspect generates new problems and where there is no stopping rule.
- *Introduced:* Day 5
