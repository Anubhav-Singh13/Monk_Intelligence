# Bibliography — Design Thinking for Product Development

Full annotated Course Shelf. Every citation used in this course appears here. When a day page says "see bibliography," this is the file.

---

## Foundational Books

### Brown, Tim. *Change by Design: How Design Thinking Transforms Organizations and Inspires Innovation.*
HarperBusiness, 2009. ISBN 978-0061766084.

The defining practitioner manifesto, written by the CEO of IDEO — the firm that popularized design thinking globally. Brown argues that DT is not a design skill but a thinking mode: empathetic, integrative, and experimental. The book is not a how-to guide; it is an argument for why the mindset matters. **Spine for Days 1–5 and Days 26–27.** Most useful chapters: Ch. 1 ("Getting Under Your Skin" — the empathy argument), Ch. 3 ("Building to Think" — prototyping), Ch. 4 ("The Sum of the Parts" — collaboration), Ch. 6 ("Spreading the Message" — integrating DT into organizations). Weakest chapter for practitioners: Ch. 7 (too abstract on social innovation for a product context).

---

### Norman, Don. *The Design of Everyday Things.*
Revised and expanded edition. Basic Books, 2013. ISBN 978-0465050659. (Original edition: 1988, titled *The Psychology of Everyday Things*.)

The cognitive science foundation for human-centered design. Norman introduces the concepts of affordances, signifiers, feedback, and the gulf of execution vs. the gulf of evaluation — the intellectual backbone for why observing behavior matters more than asking preferences. **Spine for Days 2 and 6–10.** Most useful for this course: Ch. 1 ("The Psychopathology of Everyday Things") and Ch. 2 ("The Psychology of Everyday Actions"). The 2013 edition adds a chapter on design in the world of business that is relevant to Day 4.

---

### Knapp, Jake, John Zeratsky, and Braden Kowitz. *Sprint: How to Solve Big Problems and Test New Ideas in Just Five Days.*
Simon & Schuster, 2016. ISBN 978-1501121746.

The Google Ventures Design Sprint book — a 5-day structured protocol for running DT on real product problems. More prescriptive than Brown, more immediately actionable for product teams. **Spine for Days 19–27.** Most useful chapters: Ch. 8 ("Prototype" — the fastest path to a testable artifact), Ch. 9–10 (low-fidelity methods), Ch. 11 ("What to Prototype"), Ch. 12 ("Small Data" — user testing with five people). The Monday/Tuesday protocol (problem framing and mapping) is a compressed version of the Empathize and Define phases covered in Days 6–14.

---

### Kelley, Tom, and David Kelley. *Creative Confidence: Unleashing the Creative Potential Within Us All.*
Crown Business, 2013. ISBN 978-0385349376.

The mindset book. Where Brown argues the institutional case for DT, the Kelleys (IDEO founders / Stanford d.school) address the individual practitioner's internal blocker: the belief that creativity is a fixed trait rather than a learnable behavior. **Spine for Days 1, 16–18.** Most useful for this course: Ch. 1 ("Flip"), Ch. 3 ("Spark" — techniques to overcome creative paralysis), Ch. 4 ("Leap" — bias toward action). Less useful: the later chapters focus on organizational culture, which is L2 territory.

---

### Liedtka, Jeanne, and Tim Ogilvie. *Designing for Growth: A Design Thinking Tool Kit for Managers.*
Columbia Business School Publishing, 2011. ISBN 978-0231158381.

The most concrete, tool-by-tool guide to DT at practitioner level. Structured as a toolkit rather than a narrative — each tool gets a template, a worked example, and a common failure mode. **Reference for Days 10, 12–14.** Most useful sections: the Journey Map template (Day 10), the Insight Statement format (Day 12), the POV and HMW chapters (Days 13–14). Use this alongside the d.school Bootcamp Bootleg — they complement each other well.

## Landmark Papers

### Brown, Tim. "Design Thinking."
*Harvard Business Review*, vol. 86, no. 6, June 2008, pp. 84–92.

The paper that introduced DT vocabulary to business audiences. Brown defines design thinkers by three characteristics (empathy, integrative thinking, experimentalism) and argues that DT is applicable beyond product design to business strategy and social problems. **Primary source for Days 1–2.** Free to read with HBR account; widely available through library access.

---

### Buchanan, Richard. "Wicked Problems in Design Thinking."
*Design Issues*, vol. 8, no. 2, Spring 1992, pp. 5–21. MIT Press Journals. DOI: 10.2307/1511637.

The foundational academic framing for why DT exists. Buchanan applies Rittel and Webber's "wicked problems" concept (1973) to design, arguing that the design discipline is uniquely suited to problems where the question itself is contested. **Primary source for Day 5.** Available via JSTOR with free account (limited reads/month).

---

### Liedtka, Jeanne. "Why Design Thinking Works."
*Harvard Business Review*, vol. 96, no. 5, September–October 2018, pp. 72–79.

The most rigorous practitioner-level argument for DT, grounded in a multi-year study of 50 projects across industries. Liedtka identifies four cognitive mechanisms DT activates (divergent thinking, journey-based inquiry, assumption surfacing, prototyping). **Primary source for Days 4, 25–27.** The integration argument (Day 26) comes directly from her section on "embedding DT into organizational routines."

---

### Liedtka, Jeanne. "Perspective: Linking Design Thinking with Innovation Outcomes through Cognitive Bias Reduction."
*Journal of Product Innovation Management*, vol. 32, no. 6, 2015, pp. 925–938. DOI: 10.1111/jpim.12163.

A deeper academic companion to the 2018 HBR piece. Argues DT works because it systematically counters cognitive biases (confirmation bias, anchoring, availability heuristic) that derail innovation. **Optional deeper reading for Day 5 and Day 25.** For L1 practitioners, skim the abstract and discussion; the methodology section is more relevant to L3.

## Video Lectures and Free Online Courses

### Stanford d.school. *Design Thinking Bootcamp Bootleg.*
Stanford University, 2018. Free PDF download: search "Stanford d.school Bootcamp Bootleg" — available at dschool.stanford.edu/resources. Also available as a companion to their online materials.

Not a video, but a free, downloadable field guide — the single most useful free resource in this course. Contains printable templates for the empathy map, POV statement, HMW questions, and journey map. **Used throughout Days 6–18.** Download and keep open alongside those day pages.

---

### Brown, Tim. "Designers — Think Big!"
TED2009 conference. TED.com and YouTube. Duration: 16 min. Search YouTube: *"Tim Brown designers think big TED"* — posted on the official TED YouTube channel.

Brown's main TED talk — a live demonstration of the DT mindset from the person who popularized it. **Watch alongside Day 1.** Particularly useful: the first 6 minutes, which give the "problem-finding vs. problem-solving" framing in vivid story form.

## Optional Deeper Dives

### Martin, Roger. *The Design of Business: Why Design Thinking Is the Next Competitive Advantage.*
Harvard Business Press, 2009. ISBN 978-1422177808.

The strategic-level argument for DT, grounded in Martin's "knowledge funnel" model (mystery → heuristic → algorithm). More relevant for readers in leadership or strategy roles than for product practitioners. Read after Day 28 if you want to make the organizational case for DT upward.

---

### IDEO.org. *The Field Guide to Human-Centered Design.*
IDEO.org, 2015. Free download at designkit.org/resources/1.

A 200-page, method-heavy companion guide. Contains 57 specific HCD methods with instructions and examples. Most useful as a reference during the Empathize phase (Days 6–10) and when planning your first real DT session after Day 28. Not a reading book — use it as a lookup.

---

### Luma Institute. *Innovating for People: Handbook of Human-Centered Design Methods.*
Luma Institute, 2012. ISBN 978-0985750909.

The widest single-volume facilitation toolkit. 36 methods organized into three categories: looking, understanding, making. Most useful after the course when you start running your own sessions and need method variety beyond the core five covered here.

---

### Rittel, Horst W. J., and Melvin M. Webber. "Dilemmas in a General Theory of Planning."
*Policy Sciences*, vol. 4, no. 2, 1973, pp. 155–169. DOI: 10.1007/BF01405730.

The original "wicked problems" paper (not a DT paper, but the intellectual ancestor of Day 5). For readers who want the primary source of the concept Buchanan 1992 builds on. Optional; skim the first 10 pages.
